# Batesian Mimicry in Appalachian Salamanders -- Scientific Reference

Compiled for use as ground truth in an educational field simulation. All claims sourced from peer-reviewed literature and institutional references. Quantitative values are provided wherever available to support probability tables and encounter models.

## 1. Species Biology

### 1.1 Pseudotriton ruber (Red Salamander)

#### Taxonomy and Subspecies

Four subspecies are recognized, though phylogenetic analyses have rejected their monophyly (Fisher-Reid & Wiens, 2016):

| Subspecies | Common Name | Range | Notes |
|---|---|---|---|
| P. r. ruber | Northern Red Salamander | S. New York & Ohio south to NE Alabama | Bright red-orange, black spots |
| P. r. nitidus | Blue Ridge Red Salamander | Blue Ridge Mtns of Virginia, >1500 m | Smaller, lacks black on tail tip/chin |
| P. r. schencki | Black-chinned Red Salamander | Blue Ridge Mtns, >1500 m | Strong black chin pigmentation |
| P. r. vioscai | Southern Red Salamander | Coastal Plain, SC to SE Louisiana | Purplish to salmon tones, white head spots |

Phylogeographic reconstruction indicates the ancestral population occupied Coastal Plain habitats during the Pliocene (~3.6 Mya) before one lineage invaded Appalachian uplands. The bold bright coloration of high-elevation subspecies (nitidus, schencki) appears to have evolved independently twice, suggesting convergent evolution driven by mimicry dynamics (Fisher-Reid & Wiens, 2016).

#### Geographic Range

Eastern United States from southern New York south through the Appalachian chain -- Ohio, West Virginia, Virginia, Kentucky, Tennessee, the Carolinas -- to the Gulf Coast (Louisiana, Alabama, western Florida panhandle). Elevation range: sea level to ~1500 m (Petranka, 1998).

#### Size

| Life Stage | SVL (mm) | Total Length (mm) | Mass (g) |
|---|---|---|---|
| Hatchling | 11--14 | -- | -- |
| Metamorph (after 27--31 mo) | ~40--55 | -- | -- |
| Adult male (mature) | 53--63 | 110--180 | ~10--27 |
| Adult female (mature) | 55--68 | 110--180 | ~10--27 |
| Large adult (maximum) | ~113 | ~180 | ~27 |

Mean adult measurements from a Virginia sample (n=13): 102.7 +/- 7.6 mm SVL, 164.8 +/- 11.1 mm TL, 19.7 +/- 4.4 g mass (Bruce, 2003). Total length range across subspecies: 11--18 cm (4.3--7.1 in). The tail is relatively short for a plethodontid, roughly 80% of SVL.

#### Coloration Variation

This is one of the most important variables for the simulation:

- **Juveniles and young adults**: Bright red to reddish-orange ground color with many irregularly rounded, discrete black spots. The ventral surface is lighter (pinkish or salmon). Iris is gold/yellow with a horizontal dark bar.
- **Older adults**: Coloration darkens progressively with age. The ground color shifts from bright red toward brownish or purplish tones. Black spots become larger, less distinct, and may merge. In old individuals the dorsum can appear almost uniformly dark purplish-brown.
- **Coastal Plain populations** (P. r. vioscai): Tend toward duller, purplish-salmon tones even when young.
- **High-elevation Appalachian populations** (P. r. nitidus, P. r. schencki): Retain brighter red-orange coloration.

For simulation purposes: the brightest, most eft-like individuals are young P. ruber at high-elevation Appalachian sites. These are the most effective mimics.

#### Habitat and Microhabitat

P. ruber is a semiaquatic plethodontid strongly associated with cold headwater streams, springs, and seepages. Unlike many plethodontids, it is not strictly terrestrial.

**Primary habitat**: First- and second-order streams in deciduous or mixed hardwood forests, especially near springs and seepages (Petranka, 1998).

**Microhabitat by season**:
- **Fall/Winter**: Aquatic. Found in and adjacent to deeper springs where temperatures remain relatively constant. Rarely found far from permanent water.
- **Spring/Summer**: Terrestrial dispersal. Adults move away from streams and occupy terrestrial cover objects -- rocks, logs, bark slabs, and leaf litter on the forest floor. Often encountered on roads during rainy nights.

**Substrate preferences**: Under flat rocks and logs within ~50 m of seeps, springs, or small streams. Fossorial -- known to occupy tunnel systems and subterranean crevices in the vicinity of wetland habitats. Not typically found near large streams; prefers small, cool, spring-fed water sources.

**Moisture requirements**: High. This species requires persistently moist microhabitats. It is most active and most detectable on the surface during wet conditions.

#### Diet

Larvae: Generalist aquatic predators. Chironomid larvae (36.5% of prey items), bivalves such as Sphaeriidae/fingernail clams (15.2%), other aquatic invertebrates, and occasionally other salamander larvae.

Adults: Generalist terrestrial/semiaquatic predators. Earthworms, slugs, snails, spiders, beetles (including diving beetles), springtails, and other insects. Also take small vertebrate prey -- notably smaller salamanders, including Plethodon cinereus.

#### Activity Patterns

- **Diel pattern**: Nocturnal. Takes cover under shelter objects during the day, hunts at night.
- **Seasonal pattern**: Active year-round in mild-winter areas but activity is strongly seasonal. Peak surface activity occurs in spring (March--May) and fall (September--November). During midsummer, adults are near streams. During winter, individuals retreat to spring-fed aquatic refugia.
- **Weather sensitivity**: Most surface-active during and after rainfall events. Rarely encountered on dry nights.

#### Reproduction

- Breeding: June--September; spawning in October
- Clutch size: 29--130 eggs (mean ~70--80)
- Eggs deposited under rocks or logs in or near water; female guards eggs
- Hatching: Mid-December to mid-February
- Larval period: 27--31 months (coastal populations: 18--23 mo; high-elevation: 31--33 mo; northern: up to 3.5 yr)
- Maturation: Males at 53--63 mm SVL (~4 yr); females at 55--68 mm SVL (~5 yr)
- Annual survival: ~50%
- Longevity: Up to ~20 years in captivity

#### Chemical Defense

This is critical for interpreting the mimicry system. P. ruber is NOT defenseless:

- Produces a distinct proteinaceous skin toxin called **pseudotritontoxin** (MW >200,000 daltons) (Brandon et al., 1979).
- Pharmacological effects in mice: hyperextension of hind legs, extreme irritability, hypothermia, coma, and death within 12--48 hours at LD50 dosage. Higher doses cause convulsions and death in 1+ hours.
- Toxin concentration is highest in back skin (5x belly skin, 35x gut, 18x liver, 17x ovaries).
- A single P. ruber individual carries enough toxin to kill up to **136 mice** -- comparable to the red eft.
- In human taste trials, subjects reported no noxious or repulsive taste, suggesting the defense is primarily effective against non-human predators.

The existence of pseudotritontoxin means the P. ruber / N. viridescens system may be better classified as a **Mullerian mimicry complex** (where all participants are unpalatable) with a spectrum of toxicity, rather than a strict Batesian system (where the mimic is harmless). However, much of the literature still uses the Batesian framework, and for pedagogical purposes the system functions well as a Batesian example because:
1. The newt is substantially more toxic
2. The red salamander lacks the specific neurotoxin (TTX) that predators learn to avoid
3. Predator avoidance experiments show the pattern of learned avoidance consistent with Batesian dynamics

#### Defensive Behavior

When threatened, P. ruber adopts a distinctive **coiled posture**: curling the body, elevating and extending the rear, placing the head beneath the undulating tail. This posture exposes the brightly colored dorsum while concealing the head -- resembling the defensive posture of the red eft. This behavioral mimicry was described by Brodie (1977).

### 1.2 Notophthalmus viridescens (Eastern Newt)

#### Taxonomy and Subspecies

Family Salamandridae (true salamanders and newts). Four subspecies:

| Subspecies | Common Name | Range |
|---|---|---|
| N. v. viridescens | Red-spotted Newt | NE U.S. and SE Canada |
| N. v. louisianensis | Central Newt | Central U.S. (Great Lakes to Gulf) |
| N. v. dorsalis | Broken-striped Newt | Carolinas coastal plain |
| N. v. piaropicola | Peninsula Newt | Florida peninsula |

The relevant subspecies for the Appalachian simulation is primarily N. v. viridescens.

#### Geographic Range

Throughout most of eastern North America, from the Canadian Maritime Provinces west to the Great Lakes and south to Texas, Alabama, Georgia, and Florida. Overlaps extensively with P. ruber throughout the Appalachian region.

#### Three Life Stages

This is the defining feature of the species and central to the mimicry system:

**Stage 1 -- Aquatic Larva**
- Duration: 2--5 months
- Size: Hatchlings 7--9 mm TL
- Coloration: Olive to brown-green; possesses external gills
- Habitat: Ponds, lakes, quiet stream pools
- Survival: Only ~2% of larvae reach the eft stage
- Diet: Small aquatic invertebrates

**Stage 2 -- Terrestrial Red Eft (the model species for mimicry)**
- Duration: Typically **2--3 years**, though reported as 1--7 years depending on population and conditions
- Size: 34--85 mm TL; SVL approximately 20--45 mm
- Mass: **1.82 +/- 0.71 g** (mean +/- SD, n=15, Virginia population; Spicer et al., 2018)
- Coloration: **Bright orangish-red to red-orange** ground color, with **darker red spots outlined in black** arranged in two dorsolateral rows. Up to 21 spots. This is the aposematic warning signal. The ventral surface is paler.
- Skin texture: Rough, granular (compared to smooth skin of aquatic adults)
- Habitat: Terrestrial. Forest floor leaf litter, under logs and rocks, moist woodland habitats. Often found far from any water body.
- Activity pattern: **Primarily diurnal to crepuscular** -- a critical difference from the nocturnal P. ruber. Most active during and after rainfall, especially on warm wet days. Crawls openly on the forest floor in daylight, relying on aposematic coloration for protection rather than crypsis.
- Diet: Terrestrial invertebrates -- springtails, mites, small insects, worms
- Behavior: Slow-moving, conspicuous, and generally unfazed by disturbance. Does not flee rapidly. When disturbed may adopt a curved defensive posture with tail elevated.

**Stage 3 -- Aquatic Adult**
- Duration: Remainder of life (total lifespan 7--15 years)
- Size: 7--12.4 cm TL; up to ~5 inches
- Coloration: Dull olive-green dorsally with dull yellow belly; retains the characteristic black-bordered red spots
- Skin texture: Smooth, slimy (adapted for aquatic life)
- Habitat: Ponds, lakes, slow streams with vegetation. Develops a laterally compressed, blade-like tail for swimming.
- Breeding: Late winter through early spring. 200--400 eggs per female per year.

#### Tetrodotoxin (TTX) -- Toxicity Mechanism

The red eft's chemical defense is the foundation of the mimicry system.

**The toxin**: Tetrodotoxin (TTX) is a potent neurotoxin that blocks voltage-gated sodium channels in nerve and muscle tissue. It causes numbness, paralysis, and can be lethal. It is the same compound found in pufferfish, some octopuses, and Taricha newts on the Pacific coast. TTX is also a powerful emetic (vomit-inducing agent), contributing to learned avoidance by predators.

**TTX source**: Likely produced in part by symbiotic bacteria (Aeromonas, Pseudomonas, Shewanella, Sphingopyxis) in the skin glands, rather than synthesized entirely by the newt itself. Multiple TTX analogs are present: TTX, 6-epiTTX, and 11-oxoTTX.

**Quantitative TTX data** (Spicer et al., 2018; Virginia population):

| Metric | Efts (n=15) | Adults (n=7) |
|---|---|---|
| Whole-body TTX (mg) | 0.05 +/- 0.03 (range 0.014--0.11) | 0.035 +/- 0.017 (range 0.005--0.057) |
| Skin punch TTX (ng) | 517 +/- 254 | 74 +/- 41 |
| Skin TTX ratio | ~7x higher than adults | baseline |

Key findings:
- Efts possess **~7x higher TTX per skin area** than aquatic adults (P < 0.001).
- Efts with more red dorsal spots had significantly higher total TTX (F = 17.5, R^2 = 0.57, P = 0.001). Spot counts ranged 3--14.
- Red dorsal spots themselves do not contain elevated TTX relative to adjacent unmarked skin -- the spots are a visual signal, not a toxin reservoir.
- 6-epiTTX levels were significantly higher in efts than adults; TTX and 11-oxoTTX did not differ significantly between stages.

**Population variation**: TTX concentrations vary substantially among populations (Brodie, 1968; Yotsu-Yamashita et al., 2012). Some populations have nearly undetectable TTX levels while others are highly toxic. This variation is relevant to mimicry dynamics: where models are less toxic, the benefit to mimics may be reduced.

**Predator responses to TTX**:
- Most vertebrate predators (birds, small mammals) are deterred by TTX
- Some sunfish species are not deterred by the toxin
- Some mantis species show resilience to TTX
- A Belted Kingfisher was observed beating a newt 15 times against a branch before swallowing it -- some predators can partially overcome the defense
- The emetic effect is particularly important for learned avoidance: a predator that eats one eft and vomits will avoid red-orange salamanders thereafter

### 1.3 Co-occurrence Data

#### Geographic Overlap

P. ruber and N. viridescens are broadly sympatric across the eastern United States. Their ranges overlap extensively throughout the Appalachian region, from New York south through West Virginia, Virginia, the Carolinas, Tennessee, and into northern Georgia and Alabama.

In the southern Appalachians specifically, P. r. schencki (Black-chinned Red Salamander) is described as "the most common red-orange salamander other than the eft N. v. viridescens" in the vicinity of Highlands, North Carolina (Howard & Brodie, 1971). This confirms dense sympatry in the study region most relevant to the simulation.

#### Shared Habitats and Microhabitats

Both species occupy moist forest floor habitats in the southern Appalachians:

- **Shared macrohabitat**: Deciduous and mixed hardwood forests, especially cove hardwood communities and mesic slopes
- **Shared microhabitat**: Leaf litter, cover objects (rocks, logs, bark), vicinity of seeps and springs
- **Partial separation**: P. ruber is more tightly associated with streams and springs; efts range more broadly across the forest floor. But there is substantial overlap, particularly in riparian buffer zones and seepage areas.

A field researcher turning cover objects along a stream-adjacent transect in southern Appalachian hardwood forest has a reasonable probability of encountering both species during the same survey session.

#### Elevation Ranges

| Species | Elevation Range |
|---|---|
| P. ruber | Sea level to ~1500 m |
| N. viridescens (all stages) | Sea level to ~1500 m |
| Overlap zone (southern Appalachians) | ~300--1500 m |

Both species reach their highest densities in the mid-elevation zone (~500--1200 m) in the southern Appalachians, where cove hardwood forests and abundant spring-fed streams provide optimal habitat.

#### Seasonal Activity Overlap

| Month | P. ruber Surface Activity | Red Eft Surface Activity | Overlap |
|---|---|---|---|
| Jan--Feb | Low (aquatic refugia) | Low (dormant/buried) | Minimal |
| Mar--Apr | Moderate (spring dispersal) | Moderate (emerging) | Moderate |
| May--Jun | High (terrestrial foraging) | High (active foraging) | HIGH |
| Jul--Aug | Moderate (returning to streams) | High (peak activity on wet days) | Moderate |
| Sep--Oct | High (fall breeding dispersal) | Moderate (declining with temps) | HIGH |
| Nov--Dec | Low (aquatic/subterranean) | Low (dormant) | Minimal |

Peak co-occurrence on the forest surface: **May--June and September--October**, coinciding with warm, wet conditions. Both species respond strongly to rainfall events, but note the critical diel difference: efts are diurnal; P. ruber is nocturnal. A predator that encounters both species would most likely do so at dawn/dusk transitions or during daytime rain events when both may be surface-active.

## 2. Batesian Mimicry Evidence

### 2.1 Foundational Studies

**Howard & Brodie (1971)**
- Published in Nature 233:277
- First experimental evidence that P. r. schencki is a Batesian mimic of the red eft
- Conducted at Highlands Biological Station, North Carolina
- Demonstrated that naive predators initially attacked both species but learned to avoid red-orange salamanders after negative experience with efts

**Howard & Brodie (1973)**
- Published in Herpetologica 29:33--41
- "A Batesian mimetic complex in salamanders: responses of avian predators"
- Extended the 1971 study to examine responses of multiple avian predator species
- Tested blue jays and domestic chickens as representative avian predators
- Results: Birds that had negative experience with efts (noxious taste / emetic response) subsequently avoided P. ruber -- even though P. ruber is itself edible (or at least more palatable than the eft)
- The avoidance was based on visual cues (red-orange coloration), not chemical cues
- This is the classic demonstration of Batesian mimicry in the system

**Brodie (1977)**
- Documented behavioral mimicry: P. ruber adopts a coiled defensive posture similar to that of N. viridescens, further enhancing the mimetic signal

### 2.2 Palatability and the Batesian vs. Mullerian Debate

**Brandon, Labanick & Huheey (1979)**
- Published in Herpetologica 35(4):289--303
- "Relative palatability, defensive behavior, and mimetic relationships of red salamanders (Pseudotriton ruber), mud salamanders (Pseudotriton montanus), and red efts (Notophthalmus viridescens)"
- Systematically tested the palatability of all three species to predators
- Confirmed the red eft as the most noxious species
- Found P. ruber to have reduced palatability (not completely palatable), positioning it somewhere between fully toxic model and fully palatable mimic
- Proposed that the mimicry system involves a gradient of unpalatability
- This study, along with the later discovery of pseudotritontoxin (Brodie et al., 1979), shifted interpretation from strict Batesian mimicry toward a Mullerian complex with varying toxicity levels

**Brodie et al. (1979)**
- Reported the discovery of pseudotritontoxin in P. ruber skin
- This proteinaceous toxin causes death in mice at sufficient dosage
- Shifted the interpretation: P. ruber is not a harmless mimic but rather a less-toxic member of a Mullerian complex

### 2.3 Geographic Variation

**Tilley, Lundrigan & Brower (1982)**
- Published in Herpetologica 38:409--417
- "Erythrism and mimicry in the salamander Plethodon cinereus"
- Demonstrated that the erythristic (all-red) morph of P. cinereus may also function as a Batesian mimic of the red eft
- Birds selectively avoided attacking erythristic P. cinereus, supporting the mimicry hypothesis
- Showed that in areas where red efts are common, the frequency of the erythristic morph may be maintained by mimicry-mediated selection

**Fisher-Reid & Wiens (2016)**
- Phylogeographic study of P. ruber
- Found geographic concordance: dull-colored P. ruber populations (Coastal Plain) co-occur with duller newt populations, while brightly colored P. ruber populations (Appalachian uplands) are sympatric with brightly colored efts
- The bold bright coloration of high-elevation P. ruber subspecies evolved independently twice -- consistent with convergent evolution driven by mimicry selection pressure
- Proposed a Mullerian mimicry complex as the selective mechanism driving co-evolution of color patterns

### 2.4 Color Analysis and Predator Vision

**Kraemer & Adams (2014)**
- Published in Evolution 68(4):1197--1206
- "Predator perception of Batesian mimicry and conspicuousness in a salamander"
- While focused on P. cinereus as mimic rather than P. ruber, this study provides the best available data on how predators perceive the mimicry system

**Methods**: Measured spectral reflectance from the mid-dorsal region using an OceanOptics JAZ-PX spectrometer (100 um slit, pulsed xenon lamp, QR400-7-UV-BX reflectance probe). Measured patch was 2 mm diameter circle at a constant 20 mm distance. Background spectra measured from wet leaf litter, dry leaf litter, and bare soil.

**Key findings**:
1. Mimicry is restricted to **coloration (hue)** but NOT brightness -- mimics match the hue of models but differ in luminance
2. Only **bird predators** were able to discriminate between the colors of models (N. viridescens) and non-mimic salamanders. Mammalian and snake visual systems could not reliably distinguish model from non-mimic.
3. Conspicuousness estimates were **background-dependent** -- salamanders were most conspicuous against backgrounds where they are most active
4. Birds are therefore the primary selective agents driving the evolution of mimicry in this system
5. Mammalian predators and snakes may impose selection on conspicuousness but not specifically on mimetic accuracy

### 2.5 Predator Learning and Avoidance

**Predators tested in experimental studies**:
- **Blue jays (Cyanocitta cristata)**: Key predator in Howard & Brodie experiments. Learned to avoid red-orange salamanders after 1--2 encounters with toxic efts.
- **Domestic chickens (Gallus gallus domesticus)**: Used as a model avian predator. Showed similar learned avoidance.
- **Scrub jays**: Used in the Kuchta et al. (2008) western analog study with Ensatina/Taricha.

The learning mechanism:
1. Naive predator encounters and attacks a red eft
2. TTX causes nausea, vomiting, or severe oral irritation
3. Predator forms an association between red-orange coloration and negative experience
4. Predator subsequently avoids ALL red-orange salamander-shaped prey, including the mimic P. ruber
5. The avoidance generalizes based on color similarity -- imperfect mimics receive partial protection

### 2.6 Frequency-Dependent Dynamics

Batesian mimicry is inherently frequency-dependent (Lindstrom et al., 1997; Harper & Pfennig, 2007):

- **When models (efts) outnumber mimics**: Predators encounter toxic individuals frequently, reinforcing avoidance of red-orange prey. Mimics are well-protected.
- **When mimics outnumber models**: Predators encounter palatable red-orange prey more often, weakening the learned avoidance. Mimicry protection degrades.
- **Theoretical breakdown point**: While no salamander-specific ratio has been experimentally determined, general Batesian theory and experiments with butterflies (Pfennig et al., 2001, Nature 410:323) suggest mimicry protection declines substantially when the mimic:model ratio exceeds approximately **1:1**, and breaks down severely beyond **3:1** or higher.
- In the Appalachian system, efts are generally more abundant than P. ruber in forest floor habitats (efts wander widely; P. ruber is more localized near streams), which should favor maintenance of mimicry.

For simulation purposes: a mimic:model ratio of 1:3 to 1:5 (one P. ruber per 3--5 efts) would represent strong mimicry protection. A ratio approaching 1:1 would begin to erode protection.

### 2.7 Signal Fidelity -- How Closely Does P. ruber Match the Eft?

**Similarities (mimetic features)**:
- Red to reddish-orange ground color (spectral overlap in reflectance)
- Black spots on dorsum (both species)
- Similar body proportions at similar sizes (young P. ruber vs. large efts)
- Coiled defensive posture (behavioral mimicry)
- Both found under cover objects on forest floor

**Differences (imperfect mimicry)**:
- **Size**: Adult P. ruber (100--180 mm TL, ~20 g) is substantially larger than efts (34--85 mm TL, ~1.8 g). Young P. ruber are the closest size match.
- **Skin texture**: P. ruber has smoother, more moist skin; efts have rough, granular skin
- **Eye color**: P. ruber has distinctive gold/yellow iris with dark horizontal bar; efts have dark eyes
- **Spot pattern**: P. ruber has more numerous, irregularly rounded spots that may overlap; eft spots are fewer (up to ~21), darker red, and distinctly black-bordered
- **Body shape**: P. ruber is more elongate with a longer tail; efts are stouter with a more laterally compressed tail
- **Activity period**: P. ruber is nocturnal; efts are diurnal. This reduces the probability of predators encountering both under identical conditions.
- **Coloration aging**: P. ruber darkens with age; old adults are poor mimics. Only juveniles and young adults are convincing.
- **Habitat association**: P. ruber is stream-associated; efts range broadly across the forest floor

The mimicry is therefore imperfect and strongest in young P. ruber encountered by avian predators during dawn/dusk or rainy-day conditions when both species may be surface-active. This imperfection is itself of pedagogical value for the simulation.

### 2.8 Regional Variation in Mimicry Effectiveness

**Sympatric populations** (where both species co-occur):
- P. ruber populations tend to be brighter, more eft-like in coloration
- Predators have experience with toxic efts and generalize avoidance to red-orange prey
- Mimicry is effective

**Allopatric populations** (where P. ruber occurs without N. viridescens):
- Some Coastal Plain populations of P. r. vioscai are duller, less eft-like
- Without local model presence, predators have no negative experience with red-orange prey
- No mimicry benefit -- selection may actually favor crypsis over conspicuousness in these populations

This geographic pattern -- bright mimics where models are present, dull populations where models are absent -- is strong circumstantial evidence for mimicry-driven selection.

## 3. Community Ecology

### 3.1 Salamander Species on Appalachian Transects

A cover-object search along a stream-adjacent transect in southern Appalachian hardwood forest will encounter a predictable assemblage of species. The following represents the community at mid-elevation (500--1200 m) cove hardwood sites:

#### Plethodon cinereus (Eastern Red-backed Salamander)

The dominant terrestrial salamander in eastern North American forests.

- **Relative abundance**: By far the most commonly encountered species in terrestrial cover-object searches. In many surveys, P. cinereus constitutes 40--70% of all salamander captures in appropriate habitat.
- **Density**: Extraordinarily high. Burton & Likens (1975) reported ~2,243 salamanders/ha at Hubbard Brook, NH. Range-wide estimates: 1,950 to 34,300 per hectare, median ~10,000/ha (Grant et al., 2024). Often exceeds 1--2 individuals per square meter in high-quality old-growth habitat.
- **Biomass significance**: Total P. cinereus biomass equals that of all small mammals and is twice that of all breeding birds in the same forest (Burton & Likens, 1975).
- **Morphs**: Striped (red/orange dorsal stripe on dark sides), lead-back (uniformly dark), and the rare erythristic morph (all red-orange -- itself a potential eft mimic, per Tilley et al., 1982).
- **Size**: Small. Adults 32--50 mm SVL, 80--125 mm TL, ~1 g.
- **Habitat**: Fully terrestrial, under rocks, logs, and leaf litter. Not associated with water.

#### Plethodon glutinosus complex (Slimy Salamanders)

- **Relative abundance**: Second most common large terrestrial plethodontid in many Appalachian surveys. Typically 5--15% of captures.
- **Density**: Lower than P. cinereus but still common. Widespread and relatively resilient to disturbance.
- **Size**: Large. Adults 120--175 mm TL. Black with white or silver spots/flecks.
- **Habitat**: Moist forest floor, rocky hillsides, ravines, cave entrances. Fully terrestrial.
- **Notes**: The P. glutinosus complex contains 16 recognized species across eastern North America (Highton et al., 1989). In the southern Appalachians, the relevant species varies by location.
- **Defensive secretion**: Produces a thick, sticky skin secretion that is very difficult to remove -- hence "slimy salamander."

#### Desmognathus spp. (Dusky Salamanders)

Multiple species, often the dominant genus near streams:

- **D. ochrophaeus (Allegheny Mountain Dusky Salamander)**: Common at mid-to-high elevations. Density: 0.6--1.1/m^2 in optimal habitat; biomass ~1.0 g/m^2. Terrestrial but near-stream. Often dominates calcium-poor sites.
- **D. fuscus (Northern Dusky Salamander)**: Common at lower elevations near streams. Density: ~0.8/m^2; biomass ~1.5 g/m^2.
- **D. monticola (Seal Salamander)**: Larger species associated with rocky stream margins. Sympatric with D. fuscus and D. ochrophaeus but partitions habitat by body size and distance from water.
- **D. ocoee / D. carolinensis (Ocoee/Carolina Mountain Dusky Salamanders)**: Southern Appalachian species replacing D. ochrophaeus at some sites.
- **D. quadramaculatus (Blackbelly Salamander)**: Large stream-dwelling species; less frequently encountered in terrestrial surveys.
- **Relative abundance**: Desmognathus as a genus may collectively constitute 20--40% of all salamander captures near streams. Farther from water, their proportion drops and Plethodon dominates.

#### Eurycea spp. (Brook Salamanders)

- **E. bislineata / E. cirrigera (Northern/Southern Two-lined Salamander)**: Common along small streams. Larvae are often the most abundant salamanders in stream surveys. Adults found under rocks along stream margins.
- **E. wilderae (Blue Ridge Two-lined Salamander)**: Most widely distributed species across elevations in Great Smoky Mountains (447--2025 m; Hocking et al., 2021).
- **Relative abundance**: Typically 5--15% of captures in mixed terrestrial-riparian surveys. Higher in strictly stream surveys.

#### Other Species Occasionally Encountered

- **Gyrinophilus porphyriticus (Spring Salamander)**: Large, salmon-pink to reddish species found in and near springs. Part of the broader mimicry complex. Uncommon in cover-object searches; more likely found by stream-flipping.
- **Plethodon jordani (Jordan's Salamander)**: Southern Appalachian high-elevation endemic. Can be very abundant (mid-elevation peak ~1500 m).
- **Desmognathus wrighti / D. organi (Pygmy Salamanders)**: Very small, high-elevation specialists.
- **Ambystoma spp. (Mole Salamanders)**: Fossorial; rarely encountered in surface surveys except during spring breeding migrations.

### 3.2 Realistic Community Composition for Simulation

Based on the aggregate of Ford (2002), Hocking et al. (2021), and other Appalachian survey data, a representative species composition for a mid-elevation (600--1000 m) southern Appalachian hardwood forest cover-object survey would be:

| Species | % of Captures | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| Plethodon cinereus (or regional equivalent) | 35--55% | Dominant terrestrial species |
| Desmognathus spp. (combined) | 20--35% | Higher near streams |
| Plethodon glutinosus complex | 5--15% | Large, conspicuous |
| Eurycea spp. | 5--15% | Stream-associated |
| Notophthalmus viridescens (eft) | 2--8% | Variable; weather-dependent |
| Pseudotriton ruber | 1--3% | Uncommon; stream-associated |
| Gyrinophilus porphyriticus | <1% | Rare in terrestrial surveys |
| Other species | 1--5% | Site-dependent |

Key point for simulation: **P. cinereus is overwhelmingly the most common find.** Red efts and red salamanders are both relatively uncommon encounters -- finding either one is noteworthy. Finding both during the same survey would be unusual but not extraordinary.

### 3.3 Encounter Rates per Cover Object Search

Based on published survey data and detection probability literature:

- In high-quality habitat, a searcher turning 100 cover objects might find:
  - 15--40 P. cinereus (depending on weather, season, habitat quality)
  - 5--15 Desmognathus spp.
  - 2--5 P. glutinosus
  - 1--5 Eurycea spp.
  - 0--3 red efts
  - 0--1 P. ruber
  - 0--1 other species

- Encounter rates are highest during:
  - Spring (April--May) and fall (September--October)
  - During or within 24 hours after rainfall
  - At temperatures of 10--20 C (50--68 F)
  - Under large, well-embedded cover objects (rocks > logs > bark)

### 3.4 Predator Community

Salamander predators in Appalachian forests -- the selective agents driving mimicry evolution:

**Birds** (primary selective agents for mimicry):
- Blue jays (Cyanocitta cristata) -- experimentally confirmed salamander predators
- Wood thrushes (Hylocichla mustelina) -- ground-foraging insectivore
- American robins (Turdus migratorius) -- ground forager
- Brown thrashers (Toxostoma rufum) -- leaf-litter specialist
- Eastern towhees (Pipilo erythrophthalmus) -- ground scratcher
- Barred owls (Strix varia) -- nocturnal, takes salamanders near streams

**Mammals**:
- Short-tailed shrews (Blarina brevicauda) -- major salamander predator, possesses venomous saliva. Probably the most significant mammalian predator of small salamanders.
- Northern short-tailed shrew has been documented preying on relatively large salamanders.
- Raccoons (Procyon lotor), skunks (Mephitis mephitis) -- opportunistic
- Mice and chipmunks -- may take very small individuals

**Snakes**:
- Ring-necked snake (Diadophis punctatus) -- specialist on salamanders and earthworms; one of the most important snake predators in the system
- Common garter snake (Thamnophis sirtalis) -- generalist, takes salamanders
- Northern copperhead (Agkistrodon contortrix) -- occasional

**Other salamanders**:
- Large Desmognathus (D. quadramaculatus) prey on smaller salamanders
- P. ruber itself is a predator of smaller salamanders

The significance of birds as the primary mimicry-relevant predator cannot be overstated. Kraemer & Adams (2014) demonstrated that only birds have the visual acuity to discriminate between models and non-mimics. Mammalian and snake visual systems cannot reliably make this distinction, meaning the mimicry system is shaped primarily by avian predation pressure.

## 4. Population Data

### 4.1 Density Estimates

**Plethodon cinereus**:
- Burton & Likens (1975): ~0.25 individuals/m^2, equating to ~2,500/ha at Hubbard Brook, NH
- Range-wide estimates (Grant et al., 2024): 1,950 to 34,300 per hectare; median = 9,965/ha
- In optimal old-growth habitat: up to 1--2 per m^2 (10,000--20,000/ha)
- Biomass equivalent to all small mammals combined

**Desmognathus spp.**:
- D. fuscus: ~0.8/m^2 near streams; biomass ~1.5 g/m^2
- D. ochrophaeus: 0.6--1.1/m^2; biomass ~1.0 g/m^2
- These densities apply to optimal streamside habitat; density drops rapidly with distance from water

**Plethodon glutinosus complex**:
- Less quantified. Broadly classified as "common" to "abundant" where it occurs.
- Rough estimate: 500--3,000 per hectare in good habitat.

**Pseudotriton ruber**:
- Substantially less abundant than Plethodon or Desmognathus. No widely cited per-hectare density estimates exist in the literature, reflecting both its lower density and its patchy, stream-associated distribution.
- Best available data: In Ford (2002), P. ruber constituted a small fraction of 3,937 total salamanders captured over 60,060 pitfall trapnights in southern Appalachian cove hardwood.
- Working estimate for simulation: **5--50 individuals per hectare** near optimal stream/spring habitat; much lower in upland forest away from water.
- Encounter rate: Expect **0--2 individuals per 100m stream-adjacent transect** under good conditions (wet weather, appropriate season).

**Notophthalmus viridescens (red eft)**:
- Eft density is highly variable and weather-dependent. Efts may be locally abundant on wet days and nearly undetectable on dry days.
- No widely cited per-hectare estimates for efts specifically (most newt density studies focus on aquatic adults in ponds).
- Harvard Forest long-term monitoring: Counted efts within 0.5 m of transect centerlines in 73--107 m transects to estimate minimum density.
- Working estimate for simulation: **10--100 efts per hectare** in suitable forest habitat, with enormous temporal variation. On a single wet day in peak season, a researcher might find 2--5 efts per 100m transect. On a dry day, zero.
- During mass emergence events (rare, usually spring/fall after heavy rain), densities can be much higher.

### 4.2 Detection Probability

Detection probability -- the fraction of animals actually present that a surveyor finds on a single visit -- is critical for interpreting survey data and building realistic simulation models.

**General principles** (Williams & Berkson, 2004; Bailey et al., 2004):

| Factor | Effect on Detection |
|---|---|
| Rainfall (recent) | Strongly positive |
| Soil moisture | Positive |
| Temperature (high) | Negative (animals go underground) |
| Time of day | Daytime searches > nighttime for terrestrial plethodontids |
| Cover object density | Positive |
| Season | Spring and fall highest |
| Observer experience | Positive |
| Search method | Cover object search > visual encounter for P. cinereus |

**Quantitative detection estimates**:

- **P. cinereus under cover objects**: Detection probability per visit estimated at 0.1--0.5, depending on weather conditions. Williams & Berkson (2004) found detection was significantly higher during daytime and at higher salamander densities, and lower at high temperatures.
- **Bailey et al. (2004)**: Used robust capture-recapture design in Great Smoky Mountains. Found that vegetation type and elevation were correlated with detection probabilities for Plethodon species. Temporary emigration (animals underground and unavailable for detection) was an important factor.
- **Raw counts represent a small fraction of true density**: In one study, raw counts represented only 1.4--7.2% of estimated densities after correction for imperfect detection (PMC6308888). This means the actual population may be **14--70x larger** than surface counts suggest.

For simulation encounter tables:

| Species | Detection Prob. per Cover Object | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| P. cinereus | 0.15--0.40 | Highest in wet, cool conditions |
| Desmognathus spp. | 0.10--0.30 | Near streams only |
| P. glutinosus | 0.05--0.20 | Large but cryptic |
| N. viridescens (eft) | 0.05--0.25 | Weather-dependent; diurnal bias |
| P. ruber | 0.05--0.15 | Nocturnal; low baseline density |

These values represent the probability that, given an animal is present under or near a cover object, a student searcher will detect it on a given survey visit.

### 4.3 Practical Implications for Simulation Design

1. **Most searches will find mostly P. cinereus.** This is realistic. Students should expect to turn many rocks and logs before finding a red salamander or eft.

2. **Red efts are weather-dependent.** A simulated rainy day should increase eft encounters by 3--5x relative to a dry day.

3. **Red salamanders are stream-associated.** Transects near springs or first-order streams should have 5--10x higher P. ruber encounter rates than upland transects.

4. **Both focal species are genuinely uncommon.** Finding one is noteworthy; finding both on the same survey is a good day. This rarity is itself part of the ecological story -- the mimicry system operates against a background of far more common, non-mimetic species.

5. **Seasonal timing matters.** The simulation should be set in May, September, or October for maximum realism -- these are the months with highest co-occurrence of both focal species on the forest surface.
