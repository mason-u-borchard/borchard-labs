# Curriculum Assessment: Batesian Mimicry Field Simulation

## Simulation Overview

Students take the role of field researchers surveying salamander populations along transects in Appalachian hardwood forests. The focal system is the Red Salamander (*Pseudotriton ruber*) -- a harmless species whose red-orange coloration mimics the toxic Red Eft stage of the Eastern Newt (*Notophthalmus viridescens*). Students flip cover objects, identify specimens, record morphometric and environmental data, and analyze their results to test predictions of frequency-dependent selection theory.

Target duration: 45--60 minutes for data collection, plus 30 minutes for analysis.

## Course Integration

### Placement in the Syllabus

Batesian mimicry sits at the intersection of several core topics in undergraduate ecology and evolutionary biology. The most natural placement is in one of the following units:

- **Coevolution** -- Mimicry is a textbook example of coevolutionary dynamics between predators, models, and mimics. Place this simulation after students have been introduced to coevolution broadly (parasitism, mutualism, arms races), so they can see mimicry as an outcome of reciprocal selective pressure.

- **Predator-prey interactions** -- In ecology-focused courses, mimicry often appears alongside aposematism, crypsis, and other antipredator strategies. The simulation works well as a capstone exercise for this unit, since it requires students to integrate multiple predator-prey concepts.

- **Natural selection and adaptation** -- In introductory evolution courses, mimicry is a compelling case study for demonstrating natural selection in action. Students can see that resemblance is not designed but selected for. This works especially well as a follow-up to peppered moth or antibiotic resistance examples.

- **Population ecology / community ecology** -- The frequency-dependent component ties directly into population dynamics. If your syllabus treats frequency-dependent selection as part of population genetics or community ecology, this simulation slots in there.

In practice, most instructors will find the best fit is after covering natural selection basics and predator-prey interactions, but before or during a coevolution unit. The simulation can also serve as a bridge between ecology and evolution in integrated courses.

### Prerequisite Knowledge

Students should have the following background before beginning:

**Required:**

1. **Natural selection fundamentals** -- Variation, heritability, differential fitness, descent with modification. Students must understand that natural selection acts on existing variation, not on individual "effort" or intent.

2. **Predator-prey dynamics** -- Basic understanding of how predation shapes prey populations and vice versa. Familiarity with concepts like prey switching, search images, and optimal foraging is helpful.

3. **Basic statistics** -- Students need to calculate means, standard deviations, and perform a chi-square goodness-of-fit test. If your course has not covered chi-square, this simulation is a reasonable place to introduce it, but plan for extra time.

**Helpful but not required:**

4. **Amphibian biology basics** -- Students do not need herpetology background. The simulation teaches identification features (dorsal pattern, costal groove counts, body shape). However, students who know that amphibians are ectothermic, moisture-dependent, and found under cover objects will orient faster.

5. **Frequency-dependent selection** -- This concept can be introduced fresh during the simulation. Many students will not have encountered it yet, and the simulation provides one of the best intuitive introductions available. Present the core idea in pre-lab materials: when mimics are rare relative to models, predators mostly encounter the toxic model and learn avoidance; as mimics become common, predators encounter more palatable mimics and stop avoiding the signal.

6. **Field ecology methods** -- No prior field experience is assumed. The simulation explicitly teaches transect methods, cover object surveys, data recording, and handling protocols. For many students, this will be their first exposure to systematic field data collection.

### Learning Objectives

By the end of this simulation and analysis, students should be able to:

1. **Explain frequency-dependent selection as a mechanism** -- Articulate why the fitness advantage of mimicry depends on the relative abundance of mimics and models. Recognize that this is a form of negative frequency-dependent selection: as mimics increase in frequency, their fitness advantage decreases.

2. **Explain why Batesian mimicry is an evolutionarily stable strategy only when mimics are rare** -- Connect the mimic-to-model ratio to predator learning. When mimics are rare, predators mostly encounter toxic models, reinforcing avoidance of the shared signal. When mimics become common, predators learn that the signal is unreliable, and selection against mimics intensifies.

3. **Describe the role of predator learning in maintaining mimicry** -- Understand that Batesian mimicry is not a static relationship but depends on the predator's learned association between signal (coloration) and consequence (toxicity). Naive predators, experienced predators, and predators with mixed experience exert different selective pressures.

4. **Practice field ecology methodology** -- The method is the lesson. Students learn that ecological data comes from systematic protocols: establishing transects, recording conditions, handling specimens carefully, replacing cover objects, and noting potential sources of error. This is not supplementary -- it is a core learning outcome.

5. **Evaluate data quality and recognize the importance of systematic protocols** -- Students should come away understanding that field data is inherently noisy, that detection probability is not 100%, that observer bias exists, and that protocol adherence matters for data integrity.

6. **Distinguish Batesian from Mullerian mimicry** -- Students should be able to articulate the fundamental difference: Batesian mimicry involves a palatable mimic and an unpalatable model (a parasitic relationship on the model's signal), while Mullerian mimicry involves two or more unpalatable species converging on a shared warning signal (a mutualistic relationship).

7. **Apply basic statistical tests to ecological data** -- Perform and interpret a chi-square goodness-of-fit test, calculate descriptive statistics, and use quantitative evidence to evaluate hypotheses.

## Assessment Opportunities

### Pre-Lab Questions

Assign the following questions before students begin the simulation. These are designed to activate prior knowledge and prime students for the concepts they will encounter.

1. Define Batesian mimicry and Mullerian mimicry. What is the key difference between them? Which type describes the relationship between the Red Salamander and the Red Eft?

2. In Batesian mimicry, the mimic gains protection by resembling a toxic or dangerous model. Why might this protection depend on how common the mimic is relative to the model? Think about what a predator learns from its encounters.

3. You are about to survey salamanders under cover objects in a forest. What morphological features might help you distinguish between two red-orange salamander species? List at least three things you would look at.

4. Predict what happens to a mimic population's survival advantage as mimics become more common than models. Sketch a rough graph of "mimic fitness" (y-axis) vs. "mimic frequency" (x-axis) and explain your reasoning.

5. What kinds of data would you need to collect in the field to test whether mimicry effectiveness is frequency-dependent? Be specific -- what would you measure, and how would you analyze it?

6. Field ecologists often worry about "detection bias" -- the possibility that some species are easier to find than others, not because they are more abundant, but because of differences in behavior or microhabitat use. How might detection bias affect your conclusions about mimic-to-model ratios?

7. Why is it important to replace cover objects after checking them? Consider both the organism's welfare and the integrity of future surveys.

### During-Simulation Checkpoints

The simulation should include built-in moments that prompt reflection. These are not graded -- they are pedagogical pauses designed to deepen engagement.

**Checkpoint 1: First dual encounter**
After the student has encountered both a Red Eft and a Red Salamander for the first time, prompt:
> "You've now seen both species. What differences did you notice? How confident are you in your identifications? In the field, misidentification is one of the biggest sources of error -- what would you do if you weren't sure?"

**Checkpoint 2: Misidentification feedback**
If the student misidentifies a specimen (recorded internally by the simulation), prompt:
> "Take another look. Compare the features you recorded to the key. Misidentifications happen to every field biologist -- the important thing is catching them. What feature would have helped you get this one right?"

**Checkpoint 3: Mid-survey data check**
Approximately halfway through the survey, prompt:
> "Look at your data so far. Which species have you encountered most frequently? Is this what you expected? How does the mimic-to-model ratio look at this point? Keep this in mind as you continue."

**Checkpoint 4: Copperhead encounter**
If the student encounters a copperhead (or other venomous snake) under a cover object, prompt:
> "This is why field protocols include checking cover objects carefully before reaching underneath. In real fieldwork, venomous snakes, spiders, and other hazards are a genuine concern. What safety precautions should a field researcher follow?"

**Checkpoint 5: End of transect**
At the end of the first transect, prompt:
> "You've completed one transect. Before moving on, check your datasheet. Are all fields filled in? Did you record the time, weather, and substrate for each observation? Incomplete datasheets are incomplete data."

### Post-Lab Analysis Questions

Students answer these using their collected data. Questions are ordered from descriptive to interpretive to synthetic.

1. **Mimic-to-model ratio.** From your survey data, calculate the ratio of Red Salamanders (mimics) to Red Efts (models). Express this as a simple ratio (e.g., 1:3) and as a proportion of total salamander encounters.

2. **Occupancy rate.** What fraction of cover objects you checked contained any salamander? What fraction contained no organisms at all? What does this tell you about detection probability in cover object surveys?

3. **Species frequency comparison.** Create a bar chart showing the number of encounters for each species you recorded (Red Eft, Red Salamander, and any other species). Does one species dominate? Is this consistent with what you would expect in a Batesian mimicry system?

4. **Expected vs. observed.** Published surveys of Appalachian salamander communities report typical Red Salamander-to-Red Eft ratios in the range of 1:3 to 1:5 (mimics are less common). How does your observed ratio compare? If your ratio is different, propose at least two explanations.

5. **Chi-square test.** Using published expected frequencies (provided in the lab handout), perform a chi-square goodness-of-fit test on your observed species frequencies. Report your chi-square statistic, degrees of freedom, and p-value. Do your observations differ significantly from expected values? Interpret the result.

6. **Descriptive statistics.** Calculate the mean snout-vent length (SVL) and mean mass for each species in your dataset. Report the standard deviation for each. Do the two species differ in size? How might size differences affect a predator's ability to distinguish them?

7. **Environmental covariates.** Did weather conditions (temperature, humidity, recent rainfall) or time of day appear to affect your encounter rates? Compare your early observations to your late observations. What patterns, if any, do you see?

8. **Theoretical threshold.** Theory predicts that Batesian mimicry breaks down (ceases to provide a fitness advantage) when mimics become as common as or more common than models -- roughly when the mimic-to-model ratio approaches or exceeds 1:1. How does your observed ratio compare to this threshold? Based on your data, is mimicry likely to be effective in the population you surveyed?

9. **Sources of error.** List at least three potential sources of error in your data. For each, explain how it might bias your results (i.e., would it make you overestimate or underestimate the mimic-to-model ratio?). Consider: misidentification, detection bias, time of day, weather, cover object disturbance, and spatial non-independence of transects.

10. **Seasonal and geographic variation.** How might your results differ if you surveyed in a different season (e.g., early spring vs. late fall)? What about a different elevation or forest type? Connect your answer to what you know about amphibian activity patterns and habitat preferences.

### Statistical Tests Students Should Perform

| Test | Purpose | Data Required |
|------|---------|---------------|
| Chi-square goodness of fit | Compare observed species frequencies to published expected frequencies | Species counts from survey |
| Descriptive statistics (mean, SD) | Summarize morphometric measurements by species | SVL and mass for each individual |
| Mimic-to-model ratio | Quantify the relative abundance of mimics and models | Counts of *P. ruber* and *N. viridescens* efts |
| Conditional comparison (optional) | Compare encounter rates between wet and dry conditions, or morning and afternoon | Species counts partitioned by condition |

For courses with stronger quantitative expectations, instructors can also assign:
- A two-sample t-test or Mann-Whitney U test comparing SVL between species
- Logistic regression of detection probability against environmental covariates
- Bootstrap confidence intervals on the mimic-to-model ratio

### Grading Rubric Suggestions

| Component | Weight | Criteria |
|-----------|--------|----------|
| **Data sheet completeness** | 20% | All fields filled for every observation. Time stamps recorded. Weather and substrate data present. No blank cells unless justified. |
| **Species identification accuracy** | 20% | Tracked internally by the simulation. Percentage of correct identifications. Students are not expected to be perfect, but should show improvement over the survey. Scoring: >90% = full marks, 80--90% = partial, <80% = needs review. |
| **Analysis quality** | 30% | Appropriate statistical tests selected and correctly performed. Calculations are accurate. Interpretations are reasonable and grounded in the data. Chi-square test is correctly executed with degrees of freedom and p-value reported. |

## Common Student Misconceptions

### 1. "The mimic is trying to look like the model"

**The misconception:** Students frequently describe mimicry in teleological language -- the Red Salamander "wants to" or "is trying to" resemble the Red Eft. This reflects a deep-seated tendency to attribute agency and intent to evolutionary outcomes.

**The correct understanding:** Natural selection favors individuals that happen to more closely resemble the toxic model, because those individuals are more likely to survive predation and reproduce. No individual organism is "trying" to do anything. The resemblance is the result of differential survival over many generations, not of individual effort or intent. This is worth correcting explicitly and repeatedly.

### 2. Confusing Batesian mimicry with Mullerian mimicry

**The misconception:** Students mix up the two systems or treat them as interchangeable. Some students think any two species that look alike are Batesian mimics.

**The correct understanding:** Batesian mimicry is a parasitic relationship: the mimic is palatable and exploits the model's honest warning signal. The model pays a cost because predators that encounter palatable mimics learn to discount the signal. Mullerian mimicry is mutualistic: two or more genuinely unpalatable species converge on a shared warning signal, reinforcing predator avoidance for both. In the salamander system, *P. ruber* (palatable) mimics *N. viridescens* efts (toxic via tetrodotoxin) -- this is Batesian.

### 3. "Mimicry either works or it doesn't"

**The misconception:** Students treat mimicry as a binary trait -- either a species is a mimic or it is not, and either the mimicry "works" or it "fails."

**The correct understanding:** Mimicry effectiveness is continuous and context-dependent. It varies with the degree of phenotypic resemblance, the mimic-to-model ratio, predator experience, predator community composition, season, and geography. A mimic population can be well-protected in one location or time period and poorly protected in another. Frequency-dependent selection means the effectiveness literally changes with population composition.

### 4. Not understanding that predator learning drives the system

**The misconception:** Students focus on the visual resemblance between mimic and model without considering the predator's role. They treat the predator as a passive backdrop.

**The correct understanding:** The predator is the selective agent. Batesian mimicry only works because predators learn to associate the warning signal (red-orange coloration) with a negative experience (toxicity). If predators were innately hard-wired to avoid red-orange prey, frequency would not matter. Because avoidance is learned, it depends on the predator's encounter history. A predator that has mostly encountered toxic efts will avoid anything red-orange. A predator that has mostly encountered palatable Red Salamanders will not. This is why the mimic-to-model ratio matters.

### 5. Thinking models and mimics must be closely related

**The misconception:** Students assume that because the Red Salamander and the Red Eft look similar, they must be closely related species.

**The correct understanding:** *Pseudotriton ruber* (family Plethodontidae, the lungless salamanders) and *Notophthalmus viridescens* (family Salamandridae, the true salamanders and newts) are in different families. They last shared a common ancestor roughly 150--170 million years ago. The resemblance is the product of convergent evolution driven by natural selection, not shared ancestry. This is actually a key point -- mimicry is most interesting precisely because it arises between unrelated lineages.

### 6. "More mimics means better protection for everyone"

**The misconception:** Students sometimes reason that if mimicry is advantageous, then more mimics should mean more protection -- safety in numbers.

**The correct understanding:** This is exactly backwards for Batesian mimicry. As mimics increase in frequency, predators encounter more palatable prey bearing the warning signal. This erodes the learned association between the signal and toxicity. At high mimic frequencies, predators learn that red-orange prey are often edible, and both mimics and models suffer increased predation. This is the core of negative frequency-dependent selection in Batesian systems. (Note: this logic does not apply to Mullerian mimicry, where more individuals sharing the signal genuinely does reinforce avoidance.)

### 7. Assuming field data is clean and unambiguous

**The misconception:** Students accustomed to textbook examples and lab exercises expect that field data will produce clear, unambiguous results that neatly confirm or reject a hypothesis.

**The correct understanding:** Field data is messy. Detection is imperfect -- you will miss animals that are present and possibly record animals incorrectly. Environmental conditions change during a survey. Cover objects vary in quality. Some areas of a transect are harder to survey than others. Sample sizes are often small. Results may be ambiguous or contradictory. This is normal. The skill is not in getting clean data -- it is in understanding your data's limitations and drawing appropriately cautious conclusions. This is arguably the most important lesson in the entire simulation.

## Data Products

By the end of the simulation and analysis period, each student should produce:

1. **Completed field datasheet** -- Downloadable as CSV. Should include, for every cover object checked: timestamp, GPS coordinates or transect position, cover object type and size, species identified (if any), SVL, mass (if applicable), dorsal pattern notes, substrate moisture, air temperature, and any incidental observations. Empty checks (no animal found) should still be recorded.

2. **Mimic-to-model ratio calculation** -- Expressed as a simple ratio and as a proportion, with the raw counts shown. Students should state whether their ratio falls above or below the theoretical threshold for mimicry effectiveness.

3. **Species encounter frequency chart** -- A bar chart or histogram showing the number of encounters for each species. This should be clearly labeled with axes, title, and sample size. Can be generated within the simulation or exported and made in spreadsheet software.

4. **Completed analysis questions** -- Written responses to the post-lab questions listed above. Responses should reference specific numbers from the student's own dataset, not generic or hypothetical values.

5. **Interpretation of frequency-dependent mimicry** -- A short paragraph (150--300 words) in which the student states whether their data are consistent with the predictions of frequency-dependent selection theory, citing their mimic-to-model ratio, chi-square results, and any environmental covariates that may have influenced their results.

6. **Data quality reflection** -- A short paragraph (100--200 words) in which the student identifies at least three specific sources of uncertainty or potential bias in their dataset and explains how each might affect their conclusions. This is not a throwaway section -- it is where students demonstrate that they understand the difference between textbook ecology and field ecology.
