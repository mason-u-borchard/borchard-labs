# Field Protocols: Salamander Mimicry Survey
## Pseudotriton ruber / Notophthalmus viridescens (Red Eft) System

Compiled from standard herpetological survey methods, PARC protocols, and published
field techniques for plethodontid and salamandrid research in the central and southern
Appalachians. Intended as a practical reference for researchers conducting mimicry-focused
population surveys.

---

## 1. Site Arrival and Setup

### 1.1 Gear Checklist

Pack the night before. Forgetting something means losing a full survey day.

**Data collection:**
- Rite in the Rain field notebook (or equivalent waterproof paper)
- Mechanical pencils (at least two -- they break, they jam, they vanish)
- Pre-printed datasheets on waterproof paper, on clipboard with binder clips
- Handheld GPS unit (spare batteries in a ziplock)
- Digital camera or phone with macro capability (lens cloth in a dry bag)
- Headlamp with red-light mode (dawn/dusk surveys)

**Measurement:**
- Dial calipers (plastic or stainless, 150mm) for SVL
- Small metric ruler (150mm, flexible)
- Pesola spring scale, 10g or 30g capacity (for mass)
- Small plastic bags or specimen cups (for weighing -- tare the container)
- Infrared or probe thermometer (soil and air temps)

**Animal handling:**
- Nitrile gloves (box of 50 minimum)
- Small dip net or soft aquarium net (backup capture tool)
- Specimen bags (cloth or breathable plastic, for temporary holding only)

**Site work:**
- Flagging tape (biodegradable if available -- multiple colors for transect marking)
- Metric tape measure, 50m
- Compass
- PVC stakes or pin flags for transect endpoints
- Cover boards if deploying artificial cover objects (untreated plywood, 60cm x 60cm, or half-sheets at 60cm x 120cm)

**Decontamination:**
- Spray bottle with 1% Virkon Aquatic solution or bleach solution (see Section 6.5)
- Stiff-bristle boot brush
- Plastic tub or basin for soaking equipment
- Ethanol wipes for calipers and thermometers
- Extra ziplock bags for isolating contaminated gear

**Personal:**
- Rain jacket and pants (you will get wet)
- Rubber knee boots or waterproof hiking boots
- Gaiters (copperhead country -- see Section 7.4)
- Water (at least 2L for a half-day survey)
- First aid kit with compression bandage, antihistamines, tweezers
- Insect repellent (apply to clothing, never to hands that will touch animals)
- Sunscreen (same rule -- keep it off your hands)

### 1.2 Site Selection Criteria

For mimicry studies, you need sympatric populations of both the model (*N. viridescens* eft)
and the putative mimic (*P. ruber*). Site selection should prioritize:

**Habitat type:** Mixed mesophytic or cove hardwood forest in the Appalachian range,
typically between 300-1200m elevation. Both species favor moist, cool forests with deep
leaf litter and abundant coarse woody debris. Red salamanders are most reliably found
near springs, seeps, and small first-order streams. Efts are more broadly distributed
across the forest floor but concentrate in moist microsites.

**Canopy cover:** Aim for mature or second-growth forest with >75% canopy closure.
Open-canopy sites dry out too fast and salamander surface activity drops sharply.
Measure canopy cover with a spherical densiometer or estimate by eye at each plot.

**Substrate moisture:** The single most important microhabitat variable. Salamander
encounter rates correlate strongly with soil moisture. Select sites where the litter layer
stays consistently damp -- north-facing slopes, ravines, stream margins, seep areas.
Avoid ridgetops and south-facing exposures unless you have a specific reason.

**Stream proximity:** *P. ruber* is semi-aquatic and rarely found far from water. Survey
plots should include areas within 50m of springs or headwater streams, with additional
plots at greater distances to capture the full distribution gradient.

**Access and logistics:** The site needs to be accessible multiple times per season without
excessive disturbance. Proximity to a road or trail helps. If you are deploying cover
boards, you need to be able to carry plywood in.

**Historical records:** Check state herp atlases, museum databases (VertNet, HerpMapper),
and county records to confirm both species have been documented in the area. Do not
assume co-occurrence -- verify it.

### 1.3 Establishing Transects

Standard transect design for terrestrial salamander work:

**Length:** 25-50m per transect. Shorter transects (25m) are more practical in steep or
rocky terrain. Longer transects (50m) give better coverage in gentle terrain but take
proportionally longer to survey.

**Width:** A 2m strip on either side of the center line (4m total search width) is standard
for cover-object surveys. For visual encounter surveys, a 1-2m strip is more realistic
given the density of leaf litter you are searching through.

**Number:** Minimum 3-5 transects per site, depending on habitat heterogeneity.
Place transects to capture different microhabitats -- one near the stream, one mid-slope,
one farther upslope. Randomize starting points along the stream axis if possible,
but stratified random placement is better than pure random in heterogeneous terrain.

**Marking:** Drive PVC stakes or pin flags at transect endpoints. Record GPS coordinates
for each endpoint (UTM preferred -- easier to work with than lat/long in the field).
Mark the center line with small pieces of flagging tape every 5-10m, tied at waist
height on saplings or shrubs. Use different tape colors for different transects.
Record compass bearing along the transect.

**Cover board placement:** If using artificial cover objects, place boards at regular
intervals (5-10m spacing) along the transect. Clear the leaf litter down to mineral
soil, level the surface, and lay the board flat so its entire underside contacts the
ground. Boards not in contact with the soil do not attract salamanders. Allow a
minimum 4-week colonization period before your first survey. Untreated, rough-sawn
lumber works best -- never use pressure-treated wood or plywood with chemical
adhesives.

### 1.4 Survey Methods: Comparison

Three primary methods, each with trade-offs:

**Cover object surveys** (natural and artificial)

- Turn rocks, logs, bark, and deployed cover boards along a transect
- Best standardized method for terrestrial plethodontids
- Very effective for *P. ruber*, which shelters under rocks and logs near streams
- Less effective for efts, which are more often found in leaf litter or wandering on
  the surface during/after rain
- Advantages: repeatable, non-destructive (if you replace objects), low equipment cost
- Disadvantages: biased toward species that use cover objects; misses surface-active
  animals and leaf-litter dwellers

**Active visual encounter surveys (VES)**

- Walk slowly through the plot, searching all microhabitats: leaf litter, moss mats,
  root tangles, stream edges, rock faces
- Can be time-constrained (fixed search time per area) or area-constrained (search
  until the entire plot is covered)
- Better for efts, which are often surface-active after rain, especially at night
- Advantages: captures species that cover-object surveys miss; flexible
- Disadvantages: harder to standardize; highly dependent on searcher experience,
  weather conditions, and time of day; lower repeatability

**Pitfall traps with drift fences**

- Buckets (19L) sunk flush with the ground surface along a fence (aluminum flashing
  or silt fence, ~50cm high, 15-30m long)
- Effective for sampling the full community, including species you would not find
  under cover objects
- Captures efts and *P. ruber*, plus *Plethodon*, *Desmognathus*, *Eurycea*, and others
- Advantages: passive sampling; less observer bias; good for community composition
- Disadvantages: labor-intensive to install and maintain; traps must be checked at
  least every 24 hours (daily, preferably morning) to minimize mortality from
  desiccation, drowning, or predation; requires damp sponges or leaf litter in buckets
  to prevent desiccation; risk of bycatch (shrews, invertebrates, snakes); additional
  permitting concerns

**Recommended approach for mimicry studies:** Combine cover object surveys with timed
visual encounter surveys. Cover objects give you standardized, repeatable counts of
*P. ruber*. VES during or immediately after rain events (especially at night) fills in
the eft data. This combination captures both species reliably and lets you calculate
encounter rates and mimic-to-model ratios.

### 1.5 Permits and Approvals

Before you set foot in the field:

**State scientific collecting permit:** Required in every state. Apply through the state
wildlife agency (e.g., Virginia DGIF, North Carolina WRC, West Virginia DNR). Specify
the species, number of individuals you plan to handle (even if you are not collecting
vouchers), methods, and study sites. Processing times vary -- apply 2-3 months in advance.
Some states require separate permits for each county or management unit.

**IACUC protocol:** Any vertebrate research associated with a university or federal
agency requires Institutional Animal Care and Use Committee approval. Your protocol
should describe: species, number of animals, capture and handling methods, measurements
taken, duration of handling, justification for any invasive procedures, and
plans to minimize stress and injury. For observational/measurement-only studies with
immediate release, approval is usually straightforward, but you still need it.

**Federal permits:** Generally not required for *P. ruber* or *N. viridescens* (neither is
federally listed). However, if your sites are on federal land (national forests,
national parks), you need a research permit from the managing agency. National Park
Service sites require an NPS research permit and often have their own IACUC-equivalent
review.

**Landowner permission:** Required for private land. Get it in writing. Explain what you
will be doing, when, and for how long. Leave contact information and follow up.

**Threatened/endangered species considerations:** Check state-listed species for your
study area. Some *Plethodon* species you may encounter incidentally are state-listed.
Know what they look like so you can avoid handling them if your permit does not cover
them.

## 2. Active Survey Protocol

### 2.1 Cover Object Survey Technique

This is the bread and butter of terrestrial salamander fieldwork. Done well, it is
minimally destructive. Done carelessly, it degrades the habitat you are studying.

1. **Approach:** Walk to the cover object without stepping on adjacent objects or
   compressing the surrounding leaf litter. Watch your footing -- you are in the
   survey plot, not on a trail.

2. **Lift:** Grasp the object (rock, log, board) by the far edge and lift it toward
   you, hinging it open like a book. This keeps any animals on the underside exposed
   to your view while preventing them from immediately fleeing downslope or under
   adjacent debris. For heavy rocks, pry up one edge first and look underneath
   before fully lifting.

3. **Look immediately:** Salamanders bolt. You have 2-5 seconds before a fast
   individual disappears into the soil or leaf litter. Scan the underside of the
   object and the exposed substrate simultaneously.

4. **Identify and record:** If you see a salamander, identify it visually before
   attempting capture. Note the species, approximate size, and location relative
   to the cover object. If you can get a photo before capture, do it.

5. **Replace:** Set the object back in its original position and orientation. Match
   the footprint exactly. This matters -- the microhabitat under that object
   (moisture, temperature, invertebrate community) has developed over months or
   years. Rotating a log 90 degrees is not "replacing" it.

**Timing:** Move methodically along the transect. Do not rush. A 25m transect with
natural cover objects takes 15-30 minutes depending on cover object density. With
deployed cover boards at 5m intervals, a 25m transect (6 boards) takes about 10-15
minutes if no animals are found, or 20-30 minutes with captures and measurements.

### 2.2 Time-Constrained vs. Area-Constrained Searches

**Time-constrained search (TCS):** Search a plot for a fixed period (typically 15-30
minutes per person). Record person-minutes of search effort. Advantage: standardizes
effort across sites and visits. Disadvantage: coverage depends on habitat complexity --
you may cover more area on an easy site and less on a rocky, cluttered site.

**Area-constrained search (ACS):** Search the entire defined plot (e.g., a 25m x 4m
transect) regardless of how long it takes. Record total search time. Advantage: ensures
complete coverage. Disadvantage: effort varies with habitat and searcher speed, making
cross-site comparisons harder.

**For mimicry studies:** TCS is generally preferred because encounter rate
(animals per person-hour) is a key metric for calculating relative abundance and
mimic-to-model ratios. Record both the area searched and the time, so you can
analyze the data either way.

### 2.3 Finding an Animal: Step by Step

You just lifted a rock and there is a bright red-orange salamander sitting on the damp
soil underneath. Here is the protocol:

1. **Observe first.** Before you touch anything, note what you can see: approximate body
   length, color, dorsal pattern, position, substrate type. If there are multiple
   animals, count them. You may lose one during capture, and that initial count is data.

2. **Photograph in situ.** If you can get a photo without disturbing the animal, take it
   now. Dorsal view with a ruler or coin for scale is ideal. This photo is your backup
   identification record.

3. **Capture.** Gently cup the animal with a wet, gloved hand. Approach from behind if
   possible. Do not pin the animal against the substrate -- scoop under it. For efts
   on the surface, a slow approach and gentle hand-scoop works; they are not as fast as
   plethodontids. For *P. ruber*, which can be quick, a two-hand approach is sometimes
   needed.

4. **Wet your gloves.** Before handling any salamander, wet your nitrile gloves with
   clean water. Dry gloves strip mucus from the skin, which damages the animal's
   primary respiratory and immune surface. This is not optional. Carry a small
   squeeze bottle for this purpose.

5. **Measure.** Take measurements in this order (fastest to slowest):
   - **SVL (snout-vent length):** From the tip of the snout to the posterior edge of
     the cloaca (vent). Use calipers. Gently straighten the animal along the caliper
     beam -- do not force it. If the animal is thrashing, wait. Record to nearest 0.5mm.
   - **Total length (TL):** Snout to tail tip. Use a ruler alongside the animal. If the
     tail is regenerated or damaged, note this and record TL with a flag.
   - **Mass:** Place the animal in a pre-tared plastic bag or cup on the Pesola scale.
     Record to nearest 0.1g for small individuals. Keep the bag at the animal's level --
     do not let it swing. Record as quickly as possible.

6. **Additional observations** (record while the animal is in hand):
   - Color notes: overall dorsal color, spot color, spot density, ventral color
   - Tail condition: intact, regenerated (blunted tip, different coloration), autotomized
   - Any injuries, abnormalities, or disease signs (lesions, discoloration, edema)
   - Sex (if determinable -- males of many species have a swollen mental gland, squared
     snout, or other secondary characters; difficult in efts)

7. **Record** all data on the datasheet before releasing. Do not rely on memory.

8. **Release** at the exact capture location. Place the animal gently on the substrate next
   to the cover object, then carefully lower the object back into position. Never place
   the object on top of the animal. Let it crawl back under on its own.

**Total handling time target:** Under 3 minutes from capture to release. Plethodontids
are lungless -- they breathe entirely through their skin, and handling disrupts gas
exchange and causes stress. Efts are somewhat hardier (they have lungs) but should
still be handled briefly.

### 2.4 Handling Protocols

- **Always wear nitrile gloves.** Protects the animal from lotions, sunscreen, and insect
  repellent on your skin. Protects you from tetrodotoxin (efts) and the mild skin
  secretions of *P. ruber*.
- **Wet the gloves before every capture.** Every time. No exceptions.
- **Support the body.** Hold the animal loosely in a cupped hand. Do not squeeze. Do not
  pinch. Do not restrain by the limbs.
- **Never grab by the tail.** Plethodontid salamanders (including *Pseudotriton*) can
  autotomize -- voluntarily drop their tail as a predator-escape mechanism. The tail has
  a fracture plane at the base of the first caudal vertebra. The animal will use your
  fingers as a fulcrum to break the tail off if you grab it there. Tail loss is
  energetically costly (the tail stores fat reserves) and affects locomotion and
  potentially thermoregulation. You caused it. Do not cause it.
- **Minimize time out of cover.** Every second the animal is in your hand, its skin is
  drying. In summer, a small plethodontid can lose critical moisture in under 5 minutes.
  Work efficiently.
- **One animal at a time.** If you find multiple animals under one cover object, process
  one, release it, then capture the next. Do not hold multiple animals simultaneously.
- **Change gloves between species.** To prevent cross-contamination, especially regarding
  chytrid (Bd/Bsal) transmission.

### 2.6 Distinguishing P. ruber from Red Eft N. viridescens

This is the critical identification skill for the study. Get this wrong and your data
is meaningless. Study both species before you go into the field. Handle known specimens
in a teaching collection or museum if you can.

**Six reliable field characters:**

**1. Skin texture**
- **Eft:** Rough, granular, dry-looking skin. Feels like fine sandpaper. This is the
  single most useful character in the field -- you can assess it at a glance or with
  a light touch.
- **P. ruber:** Smooth, moist, slightly glossy skin. Noticeably different in hand.

**2. Costal grooves**
- **Eft:** None. Newts (family Salamandridae) lack costal grooves entirely.
- **P. ruber:** 16-18 costal grooves clearly visible along the sides. These are
  vertical furrows in the skin between the fore and hind limbs. This is a definitive
  character -- if you see costal grooves, it is not an eft.

**3. Tail shape**
- **Eft:** Somewhat laterally compressed with a slight dorsal keel (though less
  pronounced than in the aquatic adult). Thinner and more blade-like in cross-section.
- **P. ruber:** Round in cross-section, thick at the base, tapering evenly. No keel.

**4. Body proportions**
- **Eft:** Compact body, relatively large head compared to body. 3.5-8.5cm total length.
  Stubby proportions overall.
- **P. ruber:** More elongated body, smaller head relative to body, proportionally
  shorter limbs. Adults 11-18cm total length. Juveniles overlap in size with efts
  but are still proportionally different -- more "sausage-shaped."

**5. Eye features**
- **Eft:** Eyes positioned somewhat laterally; dark iris.
- **P. ruber:** Eyes slightly more dorsal; iris has a distinctive gold or yellow tint
  with a horizontal dark bar through it. The golden eye is reliable but requires
  close observation.

**6. Dorsal spotting pattern**
- **Eft:** Two dorsolateral rows of larger spots (red/orange, bordered by black rings).
  These spots are orderly, distinct, and evenly spaced. Smaller black dots scattered
  across the dorsum between the spot rows.
- **P. ruber:** Numerous irregular black spots scattered across the dorsum with no
  organized pattern. Spots vary in size and tend to be denser and more randomly
  distributed. In older individuals, spots enlarge and may merge, and the background
  color darkens toward purplish-brown.

**Additional notes:**

- **Size alone is not reliable.** Juvenile *P. ruber* and large efts overlap in the
  40-60mm SVL range. Do not use size as your only character.
- **Chin and ventral coloration:** *P. ruber* may have black pigmentation on the chin
  region. Efts have a lighter, yellowish-orange ventral surface with scattered small
  dark spots. Check the belly if dorsal characters are ambiguous.
- **Behavior on capture:** Efts tend to be slower, more deliberate, and "walk" rather
  than sprint. *P. ruber* is faster and more likely to make rapid escape attempts.
- **Habitat context:** An animal found deep in forest leaf litter, far from water, during
  rain is more likely an eft. An animal found under a rock immediately adjacent to a
  spring run is more likely *P. ruber*. Context is a clue, not a diagnosis.

**When in doubt:** Record both possible IDs in the species field, mark confidence as
"Uncertain," photograph the animal from dorsal, lateral, and ventral views, and note
every character you observed. Resolve it later with photos and expert consultation.
An uncertain record is infinitely more valuable than a wrong one.

## 3. Data Recording

### 3.1 Datasheet Design

Use pre-printed datasheets on waterproof paper (Rite in the Rain No. 302 or equivalent).
A good field datasheet for this study has the following sections:

**Header block** (fill out once per survey session):
```
Site name: _______________  Date: ____/____/____  Start time: ______
Transect ID: ____________  Observer(s): _________________________
Weather start: Air temp ____ C  Soil temp ____ C  Wind ____
  Cloud cover _____%  Precip: None / Drizzle / Light rain / Heavy rain
  Hours since last rain: ______
```

**Observation rows** (one row per animal captured or observed):
```
Obs# | Time | CO# | CO Type | Species | Conf | SVL | TL | Mass | Tail | Color | Spots | Vent | GPS | Microhab | SubMoist | Notes
```

**Footer block** (fill out at end of survey):
```
End time: ______  Weather end: Air temp ____ C  Wind ____
  Cloud cover _____%  Precip: _______________
Total cover objects checked: ______  Total animals: ______
Datasheet reviewed: Y/N  Reviewer initials: ______
```

### 3.2 Recording Uncertain Identifications

This will happen. Especially early in the season when juvenile *P. ruber* are small,
or in poor light, or when you are tired and rushing.

- Write both possible IDs: "PSRU/NOVI?" in the species column
- Mark confidence as "Uncertain"
- Record every diagnostic character you can observe
- Take multiple photographs: dorsal, lateral, ventral, close-up of head
- Note which characters pointed toward which ID
- Do NOT guess and write a single species with "Certain" confidence. Your future
  self (or your collaborator checking your data) needs to know this was ambiguous.

### 3.3 Error Correction in Field Notes

**Never erase.** Never use white-out. Never scribble something out until it is illegible.

The protocol: draw a single horizontal line through the error, write your initials
next to it, and write the correct value above or beside it. This preserves the original
entry and creates a traceable correction.

```
Example:  SVL: 34.5  42.0  MB
                ----
```

This matters for data integrity. Reviewers and collaborators need to see what was
changed and by whom. Erasures are ambiguous -- was it an error, or did someone change
their mind about a species ID?

### 3.4 Weather Data

Record at the start and end of every survey session:

- Air temperature (shaded, 1m height) -- use a thermometer you trust
- Soil/substrate temperature (surface, under a representative cover object)
- Relative humidity (if you have a hygrometer -- useful but not essential)
- Wind speed/condition (calm, light, moderate, strong -- Beaufort scale if you want precision)
- Cloud cover (estimated percentage or oktas)
- Precipitation: current conditions and hours since last measurable rain
- Recent weather history: note if there was a major rain event in the last 24-48 hours,
  or if there has been a prolonged dry spell -- this affects surface activity dramatically

### 3.5 Timestamps

**Every observation gets a time.** Record the time you first see the animal, not the time
you finish processing it. Use 24-hour format. Synchronize watches among all observers
at the start of the day. Time stamps let you calculate search effort, document the
sequence of observations, and identify periods of high or low activity.

### 3.6 The Realities of Field Data Collection

Things that will happen to your datasheets:

- **Rain.** Waterproof paper and mechanical pencil solve this. Ballpoint pens bleed and
  smear. Felt tips are useless. Pencil on Rite in the Rain works when submerged.
- **Sweat.** Your hand will smear pencil on the sheet in July. Write small, write neatly,
  keep your hand off completed entries.
- **Insects.** Gnats, mosquitoes, and deerflies do not care about your data. You will slap
  a mosquito on your datasheet and smear an entry. Accept this.
- **Pencils.** Bring three. Mechanical pencils jam when dirt gets in the mechanism. Sharpen
  a regular No. 2 as backup.
- **Fading light.** If you are doing dusk or night surveys, your headlamp is your lifeline.
  Red light preserves night vision but makes it hard to read red ink. Use pencil.
- **Mud.** Your clipboard will end up face-down in the mud at least once per season. The
  binder clips are there so the sheets do not fall off when this happens.

## 4. Ecosystem Disturbance Avoidance

### 4.1 Cover Object Replacement

This is the most important single habit in terrestrial salamander fieldwork.

- Replace every cover object in its original position, orientation, and contact with
  the substrate. If you lifted a rock that was half-buried in soil, set it back into the
  same depression, same side down.
- Do not rotate logs. The moss, lichen, and invertebrate community on the upper surface
  differs from the underside. Flipping a log is habitat destruction at the micro-scale.
- If a cover object breaks during lifting (rotten logs do this), arrange the pieces as
  close to the original configuration as possible.
- If you accidentally displace soil or leaf litter from under the object, push it back
  before replacing.

### 4.2 Minimizing Exposure Time

Every second a cover object is lifted, the microhabitat underneath is drying out and
warming up. This affects not just the salamanders but the entire invertebrate community
that constitutes their food base.

- Lift, look, capture if needed, replace. Do not prop objects open while you process
  an animal. Set the object back down, process the animal beside it, then release.
- In hot or dry conditions, limit lift time to under 60 seconds when possible.

### 4.3 Movement Through the Site

- Walk on established paths or along the transect center line. Avoid trampling the
  surrounding leaf litter, which is habitat.
- Do not drag equipment through the plot.
- Step carefully around logs and rocks -- do not step on them (this compresses and
  damages them as cover habitat).
- Avoid walking through stream channels unnecessarily -- this disturbs sediment and
  stream-breeding salamander larvae.

### 4.4 Collection Ethics

- Do not collect voucher specimens unless your study explicitly requires it and your
  permit authorizes it. For population-level mimicry studies, measurement and
  photographic records are sufficient.
- If your protocol calls for tissue samples (e.g., tail clips for genetic analysis),
  minimize the tissue taken and process the animal as quickly as possible. Tail
  regeneration is energetically costly.
- Never remove more animals from a site than your permit allows.

### 4.5 Awareness of Reproductive Activity

- **Egg masses:** *P. ruber* females brood eggs in underground cavities near streams,
  typically in fall. If you uncover an egg mass or a brooding female, replace the
  cover object immediately and record the observation without further disturbance.
- **Nesting sites:** Some cover objects may be used as nesting substrate by other species
  in the community (*Plethodon*, *Desmognathus*). If you see eggs attached to the
  underside of a rock or log, note it and replace the object without delay.
- **Gravid females:** Handle with extra care. Do not squeeze the abdomen.
- **Larvae:** *P. ruber* larvae occupy streams and spring runs. Watch where you step
  near water. Avoid wading unnecessarily.

### 4.6 Decontamination Protocol

Chytrid fungi (*Batrachochytrium dendrobatidis* -- Bd, and *B. salamandrivorans* -- Bsal)
are devastating amphibian pathogens that can be transmitted on field equipment, boots,
and hands between sites. Bsal is particularly lethal to salamanders and has caused
population collapses in Europe. It has not been widely detected in North America as of
this writing, but preventing introduction is critical.

**Between-site protocol (mandatory):**

1. **Remove organic matter.** Before leaving a site, brush all mud, soil, leaf litter,
   and plant material off boots, equipment, and clothing with a stiff brush. Pay
   attention to boot treads.

2. **Disinfect boots and equipment.** Spray or immerse in one of:
   - 1% Virkon Aquatic solution (10g/L) -- preferred, broad-spectrum
   - Bleach solution: household bleach diluted to ~0.4% sodium hypochlorite for Bd
     (roughly 1:10 dilution of standard 4% bleach). For Bsal, use ~4% sodium hypochlorite
     (essentially undiluted household bleach or 1:1 dilution of concentrated bleach).
     The NEPARC revision now recommends approximately 10x stronger bleach concentration
     than previously advised, specifically for Bsal effectiveness.
   - 70% ethanol (for equipment that cannot tolerate bleach)

3. **Contact time.** Minimum 5 minutes of wet contact for all three disinfectants.
   Do not rinse immediately -- let the solution work.

4. **Rinse.** Rinse thoroughly with clean water after the contact period. Disinfectant
   residues are toxic to amphibians.

5. **Disinfect measuring equipment.** Wipe calipers, rulers, thermometers, and any
   instruments that contacted animals or substrate with ethanol wipes between sites.

6. **Dispose of gloves.** Use fresh gloves at each site. Do not reuse.

7. **Location.** Perform all disinfection at least 50m from any natural water source.
   Bleach and Virkon are highly toxic to aquatic organisms.

**Between-animal protocol:** Change gloves between animals of different species. At
minimum, change gloves between sites and rinse hands with ethanol between animals
at the same site. Wipe calipers with ethanol between animals.

## 5. Common Mistakes and Judgment Calls

### 5.1 Misidentification: Eft vs. Red Salamander

The most consequential error for this study. If you systematically misidentify one
species as the other, your mimic-to-model ratios are wrong and your frequency-dependence
analyses are meaningless.

**Common causes:**
- Rushing through identifications when encounter rates are high
- Poor lighting (dusk surveys, deep shade)
- Juvenile *P. ruber* in the size range that overlaps with large efts
- Not checking costal grooves (the single most reliable character)
- Confirmation bias -- expecting one species based on habitat context

**Prevention:**
- Always check costal grooves. It takes 2 seconds.
- Always check skin texture. Rough = eft. Smooth = *P. ruber*.
- If either character is ambiguous, check two or more additional characters before
  recording an ID.
- Photograph every uncertain animal. Photograph every 10th certain animal as a QC check.
- Have a second observer independently ID a subset of captures to check inter-observer
  agreement.

### 5.2 Datasheet Errors

- **Losing your place:** In a busy survey with many captures, it is easy to record data
  in the wrong row. Use a ruler or your finger to track the current row. Number every
  row sequentially.
- **Forgetting measurements:** You released the animal and then realized you forgot to
  record its mass. It is gone. There is no fix for this except discipline. Develop
  a consistent order: SVL, TL, mass, color, photo, notes, release. Always the same order.
- **Transposing digits:** 34.5mm becomes 43.5mm. Double-check measurements as you
  record them. Read the caliper twice.
- **Illegible handwriting:** Write neatly. If your handwriting degrades when you are cold,
  wet, or tired, print in block letters. Your data are worthless if they cannot be read.

### 5.3 Animal Injury During Capture

It happens. Salamanders are small, fragile, and fast. Common injuries:

- **Tail autotomy:** You grabbed the tail or the animal wedged its tail against a
  substrate and pulled free. The tail breaks at a fracture plane. The animal will
  survive but has lost energy reserves and locomotor capacity. Record the event.
  Adjust your capture technique.
- **Skin abrasion:** Rough handling, dry gloves, or scraping against rock. Manifests as
  pale patches where mucus has been stripped. Minimize by keeping gloves wet and
  handling gently.
- **Limb injury:** Extremely rare with proper technique. If an animal is wedged in a
  crevice, do not force it out.

If you cause a significant injury, record it honestly in your notes. Do not pretend
it did not happen. This is part of your IACUC obligation.

### 5.4 Weather Changes: Abort Criteria

- **Lightning:** Stop immediately. Seek shelter. Do not resume until 30 minutes after the
  last thunder or visible lightning. Standing in a forest with a metal clipboard and GPS
  during a thunderstorm is genuinely dangerous.
- **Heavy sustained rain:** Makes datasheets difficult to manage even with waterproof
  paper. Increases risk of slipping on wet rocks and injuring yourself or animals.
  However, light rain is actually excellent survey weather for surface-active
  salamanders. Use judgment.
- **Rapid temperature drop:** Salamander surface activity declines sharply below ~7-8C
  air temperature. If temps fall below this during a survey, encounter rates will
  plummet and continuing is not productive.
- **Extreme heat:** Above ~27C, terrestrial salamanders retreat deep and surface activity
  is minimal. Survey in the early morning or wait for cooler conditions.

### 5.5 Venomous Snake Safety

Copperheads (*Agkistrodon contortrix*) are common throughout the Appalachian study
range and occupy the exact same microhabitats you are surveying -- under rocks, logs,
and leaf litter near streams. Timber rattlesnakes (*Crotalus horridus*) are less
frequently encountered but present at many sites.

**Prevention:**
- **Look before you reach.** Always look under a cover object before putting your hand
  near it. Use a stick or tool to lift the far edge of heavy objects if you cannot see
  underneath.
- **Wear boots and gaiters.** Ankle-high boots at minimum. Snake gaiters or tall rubber
  boots are better. Most copperhead bites are below the knee.
- **Watch your step.** Copperheads rely on camouflage and often freeze rather than flee.
  Their hourglass pattern blends into leaf litter. You can step on one without seeing it.
- **Do not reach into crevices or holes blindly.** Ever.

**If you encounter a venomous snake:**
- Stop. Do not make sudden movements.
- Back away slowly.
- Mark the location if it is relevant to your survey (some studies record snake
  encounters as predator data).
- Continue surveying elsewhere.

**If bitten:**
- Move away from the snake calmly.
- Do not attempt to capture or kill the snake.
- Remove rings, watches, and tight clothing before swelling begins.
- Keep the bitten limb immobilized at or slightly below heart level.
- Call emergency services immediately. Know your GPS coordinates so you can relay them.
- Walk out if you can. Have your field partner assist.
- Do not apply a tourniquet, do not cut the wound, do not attempt suction. These are
  outdated practices that cause additional harm.

### 5.6 Equipment Failures

- **GPS battery dying:** Carry spare batteries. Record backup location descriptions
  (distance and bearing from a landmark) in your notebook. If GPS is dead, you can
  still reference transect endpoints that were previously geotagged.
- **Camera fogging:** Happens constantly moving between cool, humid forest and the warm
  inside of a bag or vehicle. Let the camera acclimate in a ziplock bag before opening.
  Carry silica gel packets. Wipe the lens before every photo.
- **Pencil failure:** This is why you carry three. A pocketknife can sharpen a standard
  pencil if your mechanical pencil jams.
- **Spring scale zeroing issues:** Recalibrate the Pesola scale at the start of each
  survey day using a known mass (a small fishing weight works well). If the scale
  drifts, note it in your datasheet header.

### 5.7 Marginal Observations

You glimpse a red-orange shape disappearing under a log. You did not get a clear view.
Do you record it?

**Yes -- but record it correctly.** Use a separate category: "Visual observation only, not
captured, species uncertain." Note what you saw, the time, and the location. These
observations are valuable for occupancy modeling even if they are not usable for
morphometric analysis. The key is distinguishing them clearly from confirmed captures
with measurements.

## 6. Leaving the Site

### 6.1 Cover Object Verification

Before you leave the plot, walk the transect one more time and visually confirm that
every cover object is back in place. It takes five minutes and prevents the slow
destruction of the habitat you just surveyed.

### 6.2 Flagging Tape and Markers

If your permit or land management agreement requires Leave No Trace practices, collect
all flagging tape and pin flags before you leave. Some long-term studies leave markers
in place between visits -- know your site's requirements and follow them.

If leaving markers, use biodegradable flagging tape. Standard vinyl tape persists
for years and fragments into microplastics.

### 6.3 Final Weather Observation

Record end-of-survey weather data in the footer block of your datasheet: air temp,
cloud cover, wind, precipitation status. The difference between start and end
conditions is useful for interpreting encounter rates within the survey.

### 6.4 Datasheet Review

**Do this in the field before you leave.** Sit down, go through every row, and check:

- Is every field filled in? Blank cells are ambiguous -- does it mean zero, or does
  it mean you forgot?
- Are species codes consistent? Did you switch between "PSRU" and "P. ruber" mid-sheet?
- Are measurement values plausible? A 95mm SVL for an eft is wrong. Catch it now.
- Is every observation numbered sequentially?
- Are start/end times and weather recorded?
- Can you read your own handwriting?

Errors found in the field can often be corrected or annotated. Errors found weeks
later during data entry cannot.

### 6.5 Site Disturbance Notes

Record any notable disturbance you observed at the site -- recent logging activity,
trail construction, vehicle tracks, erosion, trash, evidence of other researchers or
collectors, storm damage, fallen trees across transects. This contextualizes your data
for future visits and helps explain anomalies in encounter rates.

## 7. Post-Field Work

### 7.1 Data Entry

Enter field data into a spreadsheet or database within 48 hours of collection, while
your memory of the day is fresh. Standard workflow:

1. **Transcribe** every field from the datasheet into your database. Do not summarize,
   abbreviate, or "clean" at this stage -- enter exactly what is on the sheet.
2. **Double-entry verification:** Have a second person independently enter the same
   datasheets. Compare the two entries and reconcile any discrepancies against the
   original sheet. This catches transcription errors that single-entry cannot.
3. **Flag uncertain records.** Any observation marked "Uncertain" on the datasheet gets
   flagged in the database for later resolution.
4. **Archive originals.** Physical datasheets go into a labeled folder, organized by
   site and date. Do not discard them after entry. They are your primary record.

### 7.2 Photo Organization

- Download and rename photos with a consistent naming convention:
  `SITE_DATE_TRANSECT_OBS#_VIEW.jpg`
  Example: `STONY_20260415_T2_007_dorsal.jpg`
- Cross-reference photo numbers to datasheet observation numbers.
- Back up to at least two locations (external drive + cloud).
- Review photos of uncertain identifications with a colleague or taxonomic expert.
  Resolve IDs and update the database with a note on how the resolution was made.

### 7.3 Calculating Key Metrics

**Encounter rate:** Number of individuals per person-hour of search effort, calculated
separately for each species. This is the basic currency for comparing abundance across
sites and visits.

```
Encounter rate = (# individuals of species X) / (person-hours of search effort)
```

**Relative abundance:** Proportion of each species in the total catch. For mimicry
studies, the relative frequencies of model and mimic are the key variable.

```
Relative abundance of species X = (# individuals of X) / (total # individuals all species)
```

**Mimic-to-model ratio:** The ratio of *P. ruber* to *N. viridescens* eft encounters at
each site. This is the variable you are testing for frequency-dependent effects.

```
M:M ratio = (# P. ruber) / (# N. viridescens efts)
```

Report as a ratio (e.g., 1:3.5) or as a proportion. Calculate per site, per visit,
and across the full season. Variation in this ratio across sites with different
predator communities is the core of the frequency-dependence analysis.

### 7.4 Statistical Analysis

Common analyses for mimicry field studies:

**Chi-square test of independence:** Test whether encounter frequencies of model and
mimic are independent of site, habitat type, or season. Use when comparing observed
frequencies to expected frequencies under a null hypothesis.

```
Chi-square = sum of [(observed - expected)^2 / expected]
```

**G-test (log-likelihood ratio):** Preferred over chi-square for small sample sizes
or when expected cell counts are low. Tests the same hypotheses.

**t-tests / Mann-Whitney U:** Compare morphometric measurements (SVL, mass, color
scores) between species or between populations. Use t-tests for normally distributed
data, Mann-Whitney for non-normal or ordinal data.

**Linear regression:** Model the relationship between mimic-to-model ratio and
geographic variables (elevation, distance from stream, canopy cover) or predator
encounter rates.

**Occupancy modeling:** If detection probability varies across surveys (it does),
use occupancy models (e.g., MacKenzie et al. framework) to estimate true occupancy
while accounting for imperfect detection. This is especially important for *P. ruber*,
which has lower surface activity and is harder to detect than efts.

**Generalized linear mixed models (GLMMs):** For analyzing count data with site and
visit as random effects. Poisson or negative binomial distribution. This is the
modern standard for herpetofaunal survey analysis.

### 7.5 Data QC Checklist

Before analysis, run through:

- [ ] Are all dates formatted consistently?
- [ ] Are all species codes standardized?
- [ ] Are there any SVL values outside the known size range for each species?
- [ ] Are there any mass values that do not make sense given the SVL? (Plot mass vs. SVL
      and look for outliers.)
- [ ] Are all GPS coordinates within the expected site boundaries?
- [ ] Are there any duplicate observation numbers within a survey?
- [ ] Are uncertain IDs resolved or flagged?
- [ ] Are weather data complete for every survey?
- [ ] Do survey effort totals (person-hours) match time stamps?

## Appendix: Quick Reference -- Eft vs. Red Salamander

| Character | Red Eft (*N. viridescens*) | Red Salamander (*P. ruber*) |
|---|---|---|
| Skin texture | Rough, granular, dry-feeling | Smooth, moist, slightly glossy |
| Costal grooves | Absent | 16-18, clearly visible |
| Tail cross-section | Slightly compressed, hint of dorsal keel | Round, no keel |
| Body shape | Compact, relatively large head | Elongated, small head relative to body |
| Eye | Dark iris, lateral position | Gold/yellow iris with dark horizontal bar |
| Adult size (TL) | 3.5-8.5 cm | 11-18 cm |
| Dorsal spots | Two rows of black-bordered red/orange spots, orderly | Numerous irregular black spots, scattered randomly |
| Ventral color | Yellowish-orange with small dark dots | Lighter red, some black chin pigmentation |
| Habitat tendency | Forest floor, leaf litter, surface after rain | Near springs, streams, under rocks in moist areas |
| Toxicity | Tetrodotoxin (genuinely toxic) | Mild skin secretions (not significantly toxic) |
