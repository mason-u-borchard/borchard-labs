# Visual & Sensory Reference -- Salamander Cover-Object Survey

Field reference document for rendering an Appalachian hardwood forest
cover-object survey. Everything here is drawn from field experience,
published herpetological literature, and photographic reference of real
specimens and habitats. Intended as ground truth for simulation artists
and developers so the visuals stay rooted in what a researcher actually
sees, hears, and feels during a survey.

Primary setting: southern Appalachian cove hardwood forest, ~800--1200 m
elevation, month of May.

---

## 1. What You See When Approaching a Cover Object

### 1.1 Forest Floor Texture Layers

The forest floor is not a flat surface. It is a compressed stratigraphy
of organic material in various stages of decomposition, and every square
foot looks different.

**Top layer -- fresh litter (Oi horizon):**
Fallen leaves from the previous autumn, mostly intact. In a cove
hardwood forest, the dominant species are white oak, red oak, tulip
poplar (*Liriodendron tulipifera*), red maple, and American beech. Each
leaf decomposes at a different rate, so the litter is a mix:

- Oak leaves: stiff, curled, waxy, brown to tan. They resist
  decomposition and hold their shape for months. Edges curl upward.
  Veins are prominent. Color ranges from pale khaki (white oak) to a
  richer chocolate-brown (red oak).
- Tulip poplar leaves: larger, thinner, break down faster. By May, last
  year's leaves are flattened and pale tan, almost papery. Some have
  darkened to a wet-cardboard brown.
- Red maple leaves: smaller, palmate, decompose at a moderate rate.
  By May they are flattened, dark brown, losing their lobed shape.
  Often stuck together in mats.
- Beech leaves: pale tan to bleached white by spring, papery, thin.
  They tend to blow around and accumulate in depressions.

**Middle layer -- fragmented litter (Oe horizon):**
Partially decomposed leaf material. Structure is losing definition --
you can still tell these were leaves, but they are darker (umber to
chocolate brown), flattened, and tearing apart. Veins are visible but
the blade tissue between them is gone or soft. This layer is visibly
wetter than the top layer. It has a compressed, matted look. Fungal
hyphae (fine white threads) are sometimes visible when you peel layers
apart.

**Bottom layer -- humus (Oa horizon):**
Nearly black, crumbly, earthy organic material with no recognizable
plant structure. Feels like moist coffee grounds. Transitions into
mineral soil below. This is only visible when you dig into the litter
or look at the exposed face under a lifted cover object.

### 1.2 Twig and Branch Scatter

Twigs and small branches are everywhere, at random angles and in
random states of decay:

- Pencil-thin twigs (2--5 mm diameter): some fresh and still pale
  inside where they snapped, bark tight. Others are darkened, bark
  loosening, soft when you press them.
- Finger-thick branches (8--15 mm): often with bark still attached but
  peeling at the broken ends. Some have small moss patches or lichen
  crusts. If they have been on the ground a season or more, the
  underside is darker and damper than the top.
- Thumb-thick branches (15--25 mm): scattered more sparsely. Some still
  have small branch stubs. Bark is often cracked and beginning to
  separate from the wood beneath.

All of these lie at random angles, some crossed over others, some
partially buried in leaf litter. They are never arranged neatly.

### 1.3 Moss Patches

Moss grows in specific places, not uniformly:

- **North side of rocks**: where shade persists longest.
- **Base of trees**: especially on the uphill side where moisture
  collects.
- **Damp depressions**: swales, the edges of seeps, anywhere water
  collects or the canopy creates persistent shade.
- **Top surfaces of rocks and logs**: especially if the object has been
  in place for years.

Color range:
- Wet moss: bright chartreuse to vivid yellow-green. Almost luminous.
  This is the moss you see after rain or in the morning dew. The color
  is striking against the browns of the leaf litter.
- Moist moss: a rich emerald green, slightly less electric.
- Dry moss (hasn't rained in several days): muted olive to sage green.
  The cushions feel crisp rather than spongy. Some tips may be browning.
- Dying/dead moss: dull olive-brown, brittle, losing its cushion form.

Texture: cushion mosses (e.g., *Leucobryum*, *Dicranum*) form rounded
mounds 1--3 cm high. Feather mosses (e.g., *Thuidium*) form flatter,
fern-like sprays. Sheet mosses lie flat against bark or rock. All of
them hold water like sponges -- when you press them, moisture beads out.

### 1.4 How Cover Objects Sit in Their Environment

**Rocks:**
Typically 15--50 cm across for the ones you flip. They are half-buried,
with soil crusted around the edges and packed tight against the bottom
third of the stone. The exposed upper surface is weathered -- lighter
gray if sandstone, dark blue-gray if shale. Lichen (crustose, pale
green or gray-white) may cover parts of the top. Moss colonizes the
upper surface, especially the north-facing side. Leaf litter accumulates
on the uphill side, banked against the rock by gravity and water flow.
Downhill, there is often a small bare-soil apron where rain splashes
have washed litter away. The rock's shadow side is visibly darker and
damper than the sun-exposed side.

**Logs:**
Fallen logs in various stages of decay. A recently fallen log (class 1--2
decay) has bark mostly intact, though peeling in sheets at the ends.
Bark color varies by species: oak bark is deeply furrowed, dark
gray-brown; tulip poplar bark has interlacing ridges, gray-brown with
lighter furrows; birch bark is papery, peeling, pale cream to tan.

A well-decayed log (class 3--4) has bark mostly gone, surface soft and
spongy, colonized by moss and shelf fungi. Turkey tail fungi
(*Trametes versicolor*) form overlapping semicircular brackets with
concentric zones of color -- brown, tan, gray, cream, sometimes with
blue-green or rusty tones. The pore surface underneath is white to
pale yellow. Artist's conk (*Ganoderma applanatum*) is a single large,
hard, woody bracket, brown on top, white on the underside.

Leaf litter banks against the downhill side of logs. The top surface,
if exposed to light, has moss growing along its length. The soil
contact side is where the decay action is -- soft, dark, crumbling.

**Deployed cover boards:**
Plywood or untreated lumber, typically 30 x 30 cm or 30 x 60 cm. When
fresh, they are pale wood-colored. After a season of weathering, the
surface turns silver-gray with fine checks (small cracks) running along
the grain. After several seasons, the boards are dark gray to nearly
black on the soil-contact side, warped slightly, edges rounded by decay.
Leaves blow underneath and accumulate. Edges may be slightly lifted by
root growth or frost heave, creating small gaps. The board sits flat
on the soil but is not sealed to it -- there is always a thin gap.

### 1.5 Light

**Sunny day with canopy leaf-out (May):**
In a cove hardwood forest with a nearly closed canopy, direct sunlight
reaches the forest floor only in small, shifting spots -- "sunflecks."
These are irregular shapes, 10--50 cm across, that drift slowly as
branches move in the wind. Where a sunfleck lands, colors are warm and
vivid -- the leaf litter goes from muted brown to glowing amber-gold.
Shadows are cool blue-green. The overall effect is a mosaic of warm
spots and cool shade, constantly shifting.

The light has a green cast because it is filtered through leaves. This
is most noticeable when you look at your hands or your notebook --
everything has a faint green tint.

**Overcast day:**
Even, diffuse light. No sunflecks, no strong shadows. Colors are muted
but true -- you actually see the real colors of things better on an
overcast day because there is no glare. This is the preferred survey
condition. The forest floor looks uniformly dim. Humidity tends to be
higher, and surfaces appear more saturated.

**In a ravine or deep shade:**
Ambient light has a blue-green cast. Shadows are deep. Moss looks
almost fluorescent green by contrast. Exposed soil looks very dark.
The light level is low enough that you have to look hard to see
details under a flipped cover object -- you might use a headlamp.

## 2. What You See When You Lift a Cover Object

This is the critical moment in the simulation. The student reaches
down, grips the edge of the rock or board, and lifts it toward
themselves, exposing the ground beneath. Everything happens at once.

### 2.1 The Underside of the Object

**Rock underside:**
Dark, damp soil impression. The rock was pressed into the ground, so
its underside holds a mirror image of the soil surface -- embedded
clay particles, fine root hairs pressed flat, sometimes a thin film of
moisture. Small invertebrates cling to the underside: a millipede
coiled in a spiral, a slug leaving a slime trail, a centipede frozen
for a beat before it scuttles away. Earthworms may be threaded through
root channels pressed into the rock's surface. The color is dark -- wet
earth tones, brown-black.

**Log underside:**
This is where the decomposition is happening. The underside of a
decaying log is a landscape of its own:
- Insect galleries: beetle larvae tunnels carved into the sapwood,
  parallel grooves branching from a central channel. These are visible
  as shallow carved lines in the wood, often packed with fine frass
  (sawdust-like debris).
- Fungal mycelium: white to cream-colored threadlike networks spreading
  across the wood surface. Sometimes dense enough to form a visible mat.
  In some places, rhizomorphs (thick cord-like mycelial strands) run
  along the wood's surface, looking like dark brown or black shoestrings.
- Moisture: beading on the wood surface, especially if it has rained
  recently. The wood is dark from saturation.
- Pill bugs and sowbugs clinging to the surface in clusters.

**Board underside:**
Darkened from moisture, often with soil particles stuck to the surface.
Fine root hairs may be attached. Slugs are common. The wood surface may
have a slight greenish cast from algal growth. Moisture is beaded or
filmed across the surface. After several seasons, the underside is soft
and beginning to delaminate.

### 2.2 The Exposed Ground Beneath

The soil under a cover object is visually distinct from the surrounding
forest floor. This contrast is important for rendering:

- **Color**: noticeably darker than surrounding surface soil. The cover
  object has kept this patch in permanent shade and trapped moisture.
  The soil is wet, dark brown to nearly black. It contrasts sharply with
  the lighter, drier surface soil around it.
- **Texture**: smooth, slightly shiny from moisture. You can see
  individual soil aggregates. Earthworm channels appear as small holes
  (2--4 mm diameter) at the surface. Sometimes you see the dark, coiled
  castings of earthworms deposited at the surface.
- **Invertebrates**: this is the most visually active part. Pill bugs
  (isopods, *Armadillidium vulgare*) immediately curl into balls -- gray
  armored spheres about 5--8 mm across. Centipedes (reddish-brown,
  fast, many-legged) make rapid lateral escapes. Beetle larvae (white,
  C-shaped grubs) may be exposed in the soil. Ground beetles (dark,
  shiny, fast) scramble for cover. Ants scatter. Springtails (tiny,
  pale, jumping) are sometimes so numerous they look like the soil is
  alive.
- **Root exposure**: fine root hairs (hair-thin, pale, almost white)
  are exposed where the object was resting. They form a delicate
  network across the soil surface.
- **Moisture sheen**: in humid conditions, the exposed soil has a slight
  sheen -- not puddled water, but a surface film that catches light.

### 2.3 The Contrast

The visual impact of lifting a cover object is the contrast between the
dark, wet, biologically active microhabitat beneath and the drier,
lighter, less active surface around it. It is like opening a small door
into a different world. The boundary is sharp -- within 1--2 cm of the
object's edge, the soil transitions from dark/wet to lighter/drier.

In the simulation, this contrast needs to read immediately. The patch
under the cover object should be visibly darker and moister than the
surrounding forest floor.

### 2.4 When You Find Nothing

This is most flips. You lift the rock or board and see: dark wet soil,
some invertebrates scrambling, maybe a worm or two. No salamander.
The visual is anticlimactic on purpose -- wet dirt and bugs. You scan
the exposed patch for 2--3 seconds, see nothing of interest, replace
the object, and move to the next one.

The simulation needs to convey this: most flips are boring. That is
what makes the finds exciting.

### 2.5 When You Find a Salamander

The flash of color or movement is immediate. Your eye catches it
before your brain processes what species it is. Here is what each
encounter looks like:

**Red-backed salamander (*Plethodon cinereus*), striped morph:**
A slender, dark body with a copper-red to brick-red dorsal stripe
running from the base of the head to the tail. The stripe is broad --
about one-third the body width. The flanks and head are dark gray to
black with fine silver-white speckling. The belly is a salt-and-pepper
pattern of black and white. The animal is pressed flat against the soil,
motionless. For 1--3 seconds, it does not move at all (freeze
response). Then it begins a slow, deliberate crawl -- not a sprint,
just a steady movement away from the light. It may undulate its tail
as a distraction.

**Red-backed salamander, lead-backed morph:**
Same body shape and behavior, but no red stripe. The entire dorsum is
uniform dark gray to charcoal, sometimes with faint brassy flecking.
The belly pattern is the same salt-and-pepper. Against the dark soil,
the lead-backed morph is harder to spot -- it blends in. You might miss
it entirely if it does not move.

**Red eft (*Notophthalmus viridescens*):**
Bright orange-red, immediately conspicuous against the dark earth. The
color is so vivid it looks almost artificial -- like someone dropped a
piece of candy on the ground. The body is stout, 3--5 cm long, with
rough granular skin (like fine sandpaper). Two dorsolateral rows of
black-bordered red spots run along the back. The belly is paler, a
dull yellow-orange. The eft is not in a hurry. It relies on its
toxicity, not speed, for defense. It may sit motionless or walk slowly,
deliberately, with a stiff-legged gait. It does not flee.

**Red salamander (*Pseudotriton ruber*):**
Bright red-orange (young individuals) to dark reddish-brown (old
adults). Smooth, glossy skin that catches the light -- wet-looking
even when the skin is not especially wet. The body is stout and
muscular, noticeably larger than an eft. Scattered black spots cover
the dorsum -- irregular in size and shape, some overlapping, no distinct
borders. When disturbed, the red salamander assumes a defensive posture:
the body curls, the tail is raised over the head and undulated slowly
from side to side, and the head is tucked under the base of the tail.
This posture exposes the bright red dorsum and makes the animal look
more like a toxic eft -- this is the Batesian mimicry in action.

The gold-yellow iris is visible if you look at the eyes -- a warm ring
of amber-gold around the dark pupil, distinct from the dark eyes of
most other species. This is one of the key diagnostic features.

**Slimy salamander (*Plethodon glutinosus*):**
Jet black body covered in silver-white to brassy speckles and flecks.
The flecks increase in density along the sides, sometimes forming
irregular white bands on the lower flanks. Against the dark soil, the
black body can be hard to see -- the white flecks are what catch your
eye. When handled, the skin secretes a thick, sticky, glue-like
substance that adheres to your gloves and is nearly impossible to
remove. This is the most memorable tactile experience in Appalachian
salamander work.

## 3. The Close-Up Examination (In-Hand View)

After capture, the salamander is held in a wet-gloved hand for
measurement and identification.

### 3.1 The Gloved Hand

Blue nitrile gloves, wetted before handling (dry gloves can damage
amphibian skin). Water droplets bead on the glove surface. The glove
color provides consistent background contrast for viewing the animal --
bright blue against the animal's body colors. This is a distinctive
visual of field herpetology.

### 3.2 Handling Position

The animal is held gently in a loosely cupped palm, not pinched or
squeezed. The researcher tilts their hand to view:
- Dorsal surface: looking down at the back, checking stripe/spot
  pattern, overall color, tail condition.
- Lateral profile: tilting to see the flanks, costal grooves, body
  proportions.
- Ventral surface: carefully turning the animal or using a second hand
  to tilt it, checking belly pattern, chin coloration, counting toe
  tips.

The animal is active in the hand -- it walks, tries to squeeze between
fingers, pushes with its head. Small species can be contained easily.
Larger species (adult *P. ruber*, *P. glutinosus*) are more of a
handful and may curl their tails around your fingers.

### 3.3 Size Reference

- **Red eft**: fits easily on two extended fingers. 3--5 cm snout-vent
  length, 5.5--9 cm total length. A small, stocky animal.
- **Red-backed salamander**: about as long as your palm. 3--5 cm SVL,
  6.5--12.5 cm total. Slender-bodied.
- **Red salamander**: drapes across your entire hand. 3.5--8 cm SVL,
  8--18 cm total. Stout, heavy-bodied. Feels substantial.
- **Slimy salamander**: similar to the red salamander in size. 4.5--8.5
  cm SVL, 10.5--19.5 cm total. The sticky secretion makes it feel
  heavier than it is.

### 3.4 Diagnostic Features at Close Range (~15 cm / 6 inches)

**Costal grooves (plethodontids):**
Subtle vertical creases in the skin along the flanks, running from the
dorsal midline to the ventral surface. They look like fine score lines
pressed into the skin. On a live, moist animal, they are visible but
not dramatic -- you have to look for them. They are easier to see on
the lateral view. Count: *P. cinereus* has 18--20 (usually 19),
*P. ruber* has 16--18 (usually 17), *P. glutinosus* has 15--17
(usually 16). Counting them on a wriggling live animal is not easy.

**Nasolabial grooves (plethodontids):**
Fine grooves running from the nostril down to the edge of the upper
lip. Visible at very close range as faint lines. Diagnostic for family
Plethodontidae (lungless salamanders). The eft (family Salamandridae)
does not have them -- this is one way to tell the families apart, but
it requires close examination with good light.

**Skin texture -- red eft:**
Rough, dry, granular. Under close examination, the skin looks like
very fine sandpaper or stippled leather. The granules are tiny raised
bumps covering the entire dorsal surface. This texture is strikingly
different from the smooth plethodontids. The roughness is why the eft
feels "dry" compared to other salamanders, even though it is still a
moist-skinned amphibian.

**Skin texture -- plethodontids (*P. cinereus*, *P. ruber*, *P. glutinosus*):**
Smooth, moist, slightly shiny. Light reflects off the surface in a
diffuse sheen. *P. ruber* in particular has a glossy appearance -- the
smooth wet skin catches light and creates small specular highlights.
*P. glutinosus* has an additional wet-looking quality from its sticky
secretion.

**Eft spots:**
Black-bordered red-orange spots arranged in two dorsolateral rows.
Each spot is distinct and well-defined -- a red-orange center
surrounded by a thin black ring. The spots are orderly, almost
decorative. They look printed on, not blended. Typically 3--5 spots
per row on each side.

**Red salamander spots:**
Scattered black spots of irregular size, shape, and distribution across
the red-orange dorsum. No borders. Some spots are round, some elongated,
some overlapping. In young individuals the spots are small and sparse
against a bright red background. In old adults the spots enlarge, merge,
and the background darkens to a purplish-brown, making the whole animal
look muddy and dark. The spot pattern is asymmetric -- left side does
not match right side.

**Red salamander iris:**
The gold-yellow iris is one of the most useful field marks. At close
range, you see a ring of warm amber-yellow around the dark round pupil.
The iris catches light and appears to glow slightly. This is visually
distinct from the dark brown or black irises of *Desmognathus* species
and the uniformly dark eyes of *P. cinereus*.

## 4. Environmental Atmosphere -- Appalachian Cove Hardwood Forest in May

### 4.1 Visual Atmosphere

The canopy is nearly closed. Tulip poplar, white oak, red oak, sugar
maple, basswood, and yellow buckeye form the overstory at 25--35 m.
Their leaves are fully expanded by mid-May, creating a continuous green
ceiling. The light that reaches the forest floor is filtered green --
everything has a cool, aqueous quality.

**Understory**: sparse, because so little light penetrates. Shade-
tolerant species: dogwood (*Cornus florida*) with white bracts fading
by late May, spicebush (*Lindera benzoin*) with small yellow-green
leaves, pawpaw (*Asimina triloba*) with large drooping leaves. In some
areas, dense thickets of great rhododendron (*Rhododendron maximum*)
with dark, leathery, evergreen leaves create walls of green along
stream margins. Mountain laurel (*Kalmia latifolia*) is beginning to
bud.

**Herbaceous layer**: spring ephemeral wildflowers are mostly finished
by mid-May. You see the fading remnants: trillium flowers are going to
seed, their white petals turned pink and drooping. Bloodroot leaves
are large and green but flowers are long gone. Jack-in-the-pulpit
(*Arisaema triphyllum*) is still upright -- its hooded green-and-brown
striped spathe is distinctive. Ferns are unfurling -- Christmas fern
(*Polystichum acrostichoides*) is evergreen and upright, while wood
ferns and maidenhair ferns are pushing up new fiddle-heads (coiled
fronds, covered in silver-brown scales).

**General visual impression**: a cathedral of green columns (tree
trunks) rising into a green ceiling, with a brown floor. The dominant
colors are green (canopy, moss, ferns) and brown (leaf litter, bark,
soil). Contrast is low on overcast days, higher on sunny days when
sunflecks create dramatic spots of warm light.

### 4.2 Sound

The soundscape is constant and layered. You never survey in silence.

**Bird chorus (dominant, continuous):**
- Wood thrush (*Hylocichla mustelina*): the signature sound of
  Appalachian cove forests. A flute-like, liquid song --
  "ee-oh-LAY" -- with harmonics that ring through the understory.
  Often sings from a mid-canopy perch. Heard most in early morning and
  evening.
- Ovenbird (*Seiurus aurocapilla*): a loud, emphatic chant from the
  forest floor or low branches -- "TEACH-er TEACH-er TEACH-er" --
  rising in volume. One of the most common sounds in deciduous forest
  in May.
- Red-eyed vireo (*Vireo olivaceus*): a continuous, monotonous
  phrasing from the canopy. Short phrases, each slightly different,
  repeated all day long without pause. Sometimes called "the preacher"
  because it never stops talking.
- Carolina wren (*Thryothorus ludovicianus*): loud, rolling
  "tea-KETTLE tea-KETTLE tea-KETTLE" or similar three-note phrases.
  Disproportionately loud for a small bird.
- Scarlet tanager (*Piranga olivacea*): a burry, robin-like song from
  the canopy. Distinctive sharp "chip-BURR" call note.

**Background sounds:**
- Stream: not rushing, more a steady murmur -- water trickling over
  rocks and small ledges. In a cove forest, you are usually within
  earshot of a stream. The sound is constant, and after a while you
  stop noticing it.
- Wind in canopy: a slow, continuous swoosh, like distant surf. Leaves
  rustle. The sound rises and falls as gusts pass through.
- Woodpecker drumming: occasional rapid bursts, resonant, echoing.
  Pileated woodpecker drums are deep and powerful. Downy woodpecker
  drums are lighter and faster.

**Incidental sounds:**
- Your own footsteps crunching leaf litter.
- A startled chipmunk chipping alarm calls.
- A log shifting when you lift it, creaking, sometimes breaking.
- A raven croaking overhead.

### 4.3 Humidity and Moisture

Everything is damp. Relative humidity in a cove forest in May typically
runs 80--95% at ground level, especially in the morning.

- Your field notebook paper gets soft and wavy. Pencil works better
  than pen (ink smears).
- Your camera lens fogs the instant you pull it out of its bag (the
  glass is cooler than the humid air). You learn to leave the bag open
  for 10 minutes before you need to shoot.
- Sweat does not evaporate -- it beads on your skin and soaks into your
  shirt collar. By mid-morning you are damp all over.
- Cover objects are wet to the touch, even if it has not rained in two
  days. The underside is always wet.
- Moss squelches when you step on it.

### 4.4 Smell

The dominant smell is decomposition -- not rot, not unpleasant, but a
rich, earthy, fungal scent. Components:

- Decaying leaf litter: a deep humus smell, like potting soil but more
  complex. Slightly sweet.
- Fungal notes: a mushroom-like undertone, especially near rotting logs.
  Sometimes you can smell a specific mushroom colony before you see it.
- After rain: petrichor (the geosmin compound released by soil bacteria)
  intensified tenfold by the thick litter layer. This is the most
  memorable forest smell -- damp earth and decomposition amplified.
- Rhododendron: if you brush against the leaves, a faint resinous
  scent.
- Occasionally: the sharp, slightly chemical smell of a slimy
  salamander's skin secretion on your gloves.

### 4.5 Insects and Arachnids

- **Gnats**: small clouds of them hover at head height, especially near
  seeps and wet areas. They orbit your face, drawn by CO2 and moisture.
  Not biting (usually fungus gnats), but distracting.
- **Mosquitoes**: present at dawn and dusk, less active midday. You hear
  the whine before you feel the bite.
- **Ticks**: you do not see them until you find one crawling on your
  pants or embedded in your skin at the end of the day. Lone star ticks
  (*Amblyomma americanum*) and black-legged ticks (*Ixodes scapularis*)
  are both present. You check your pants legs every 30 minutes.
- **Spider webs**: strung between saplings, ferns, and low branches.
  In the morning they catch dew and are visible as silver threads.
  You walk through them constantly. Orb-weaver webs span gaps at
  face height.
- **Under cover objects**: the invertebrate community described in
  Section 2.2 -- pill bugs, centipedes, beetles, ants, springtails,
  millipedes, slugs, earthworms.

## 6. Summary of Key Visual Contrasts for Rendering

These are the visual contrasts that matter most for the simulation to
read as authentic:

1. **Soil under cover object vs. surrounding soil**: dark/wet vs.
   light/dry. This is the biggest contrast in the flip moment.

2. **Red-orange salamanders vs. dark soil**: the eft and young red
   salamander "pop" against the dark exposed soil. This is what makes
   those finds visually striking.

3. **Wet moss vs. dry leaf litter**: bright green against muted brown.
   Moss patches should read as vivid color accents on the forest floor.

4. **Sunflecks vs. shade**: on sunny days, warm gold spots on cool
   green-brown ground. This is the most dynamic visual element of the
   forest floor.

5. **Eft rough skin vs. plethodontid smooth skin**: texture difference
   visible in the close-up examination view. The eft should look matte
   and granular; plethodontids should look glossy and smooth.

6. **Eft bordered spots vs. red salamander unbordered spots**: the eft's
   spots are neat circles with black borders. The red salamander's
   spots are irregular blotches without borders. This is the key
   visual distinction students must learn.

7. **Young red salamander vs. old red salamander**: bright vivid red
   vs. dark purplish-brown. The ontogenetic color change is dramatic.
   Young individuals closely resemble the eft (the mimicry); old
   individuals do not.

8. **Gold iris (P. ruber) vs. dark iris (everything else)**: a small
   but critical detail in the close-up view. The gold eye is the most
   reliable quick diagnostic.

## 7. Notes on What's Missing from Most Illustrations

Things that field researchers see but artists often leave out:

- **Soil stuck to the salamander's body**: especially on the ventral
  surface and around the legs. Dark soil particles against the skin.
- **Moisture film on everything**: the wet sheen on soil, wood, rock,
  and skin. Nothing in a cove forest in May is completely dry.
- **The mess of the forest floor**: real forest floors are chaotic.
  Leaves overlapping at every angle, twigs crossed, patches of bare
  soil, moss growing in irregular patches. No repeating patterns.
  No neat tiling.
- **The invertebrate scatter**: when you flip a cover object, there
  are bugs. Always. Pill bugs curling, centipedes running, ants
  scattering. This adds life to the scene.
- **Color variation within species**: no two individual salamanders
  are exactly the same color. The hex values above are central
  tendencies. Each rendered individual should have slight random
  offsets in hue and saturation.
- **The shadow under your hand**: when you lift a cover object, your
  hand and arm cast a shadow over the exposed patch. The light
  changes as you move.
- **Dirty gloves**: by the third cover object, your blue nitrile
  gloves have soil smeared on the fingertips and palm. They are not
  pristine.
